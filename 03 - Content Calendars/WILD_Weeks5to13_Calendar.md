## Day 30 — Tuesday
**Theme:** The galantamine question — evidence, risks, and reality

**⬡ BIO — X thread (6 posts):**
> Post 1: "The most asked supplement question: does galantamine work for lucid dreaming? Here's the evidence."
> Post 2: Galantamine is an acetylcholinesterase inhibitor — it prevents the breakdown of acetylcholine, the neurotransmitter elevated during REM and waking focused attention. More ACh = more vivid, more memorable, more cognitively active dreams.
> Post 3: The research. Two controlled studies (LaBerge 2004, Stumbrys 2012) found significantly higher rates of lucid dreaming with galantamine vs placebo, particularly when combined with WBTB. Effect sizes were substantial — not marginal.
> Post 4: The protocol. 4–8mg, taken during the WBTB window (not at bedtime — this would suppress REM). Not every night — cycling prevents tolerance. One study found 3 consecutive nights maximum before a 4-night break.
> Post 5: The risks. Galantamine is a pharmaceutical (used clinically for Alzheimer's). Side effects at high doses: nausea, vivid nightmares, over-stimulation. At 4mg in healthy adults, side effects are rare but not zero. Not suitable for everyone.
> Post 6: My recommendation: it's a tool, not a crutch. Master the non-supplemented technique first. Then use galantamine to verify your protocol works — if it doesn't work with pharmaceutical-grade cholinergic support, the timing or technique is wrong, not the supplement.
