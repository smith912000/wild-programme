HRV and Sleep Tracking — Your Readiness Score

Video lesson Coming soon — check back after launch
~14 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Explain HRV as a metric for autonomic nervous system flexibility and understand why it predicts deep sleep readiness.
  • Describe how to interpret HRV trends rather than individual nights, and what trend direction indicates about system state.
  • Define the three-component readiness score and explain why each variable was selected.
  • Identify wearable options for readiness tracking and understand the limitations of consumer-grade devices.
  • Apply your readiness score data to make binary decisions about WILD practice readiness on any given night.

Lesson Content

Every tool discussed in this Body track — nutrition, meal timing, glucose management, gut health, inflammation reduction, exercise timing, supplementation — produces effects that are ultimately visible in one place: your data. The body is a system, and systems have measurable state variables. The question is which variables to measure, how to read them, and what to do when they tell you something you do not want to hear.

This readiness score is not a concept unique to lucid dreaming. It is an adaptation of a fundamental idea from systems biology: controllability rank, the degree to which a system can be driven to a desired state from its current state. A high-readiness system can navigate to deep, architecturally clean sleep reliably, on demand. A low-readiness system cannot — regardless of what techniques are applied.

HRV: The Primary Metric

Heart rate variability is the variation in time between consecutive heartbeats. It is measured in milliseconds (ms) and typically expressed as RMSSD (root mean square of successive differences) for short-term recordings, or SDNN (standard deviation of normal-to-normal intervals) for longer ones. Most consumer wearables report a simplified HRV score based on RMSSD.

HRV is the single most information-rich metric produced by a wearable device for sleep readiness purposes. Here is why. The interval between heartbeats is modulated by the autonomic nervous system — specifically by the balance between sympathetic (activating) and parasympathetic (recovering) branches. High HRV indicates that the parasympathetic system is dominant and the autonomic system has flexibility — it can respond rapidly to demands in either direction. Low HRV indicates sympathetic dominance: the system is under load, either recovering from a stressor (training, illness, alcohol, poor sleep, psychological stress) or in a chronically impaired state.

For sleep specifically: parasympathetic dominance is a prerequisite for SWS initiation and maintenance. The VLPO neurons that drive sleep onset are activated by GABA and adenosine under conditions of parasympathetic tone. High HRV before bed correlates with faster sleep onset, deeper SWS in the first half of the night, and higher sleep efficiency — all conditions that produce more robust REM in the second half. Low HRV before bed correlates with the opposite.

The predictive power is not trivial. Studies using pre-sleep HRV to predict sleep architecture find correlations between HRV and SWS duration of r=0.6-0.7 — stronger than any subjective sleepiness measure and comparable to laboratory polysomnography-derived predictions.

Reading Trends, Not Points

The most common misuse of HRV data is treating individual nights as meaningful. A single HRV reading is noisy — it varies with body position, breathing pattern, meal timing on that day, ambient temperature, and dozens of other variables. The signal is in the trend.

Read your HRV across a rolling 7-day average. What matters is:

Trend direction: A rising 7-day HRV average indicates that recovery is outpacing stress — the system is building control capacity. A falling 7-day average indicates accumulated stress load — a signal to reduce training, improve nutrition, and prioritise sleep extension before attempting demanding practices.

Deviation from personal baseline: Your HRV baseline is individual and determined by genetics, age, fitness level, and training history. A 35-year-old recreational athlete might baseline at 55ms; an elite endurance athlete at 90ms. What matters is not the absolute number but deviation from your own 30-day rolling average. A morning reading 10% below your baseline is a low-readiness signal regardless of whether your absolute number appears "good" by population norms.

Trend over the training cycle: If you are using resistance training or HIIT as part of your exercise protocol (as recommended in B6), expect HRV to dip 5-10% in the 24-48 hours after a hard session and recover within 48-72 hours. This is normal adaptation — the hormetic response in progress. HRV that fails to recover to baseline within 72 hours is a sign of insufficient recovery bandwidth.

The Three Readiness Variables

Your readiness score is a simplified, practical composite of three variables that together describe the system's current navigability:

1. HRV (7-day rolling average, normalised to personal baseline). Scored as: above baseline = +1, at baseline ± 5% = 0, below baseline >5% = -1.

2. Glucose variability (nocturnal, prior night). If using CGM: a stable overnight trace (variation <2 mmol/L) = +1, moderate variation (2-3 mmol/L) = 0, high variation (>3 mmol/L, nocturnal trough below 4.0) = -1. If not using CGM: use nocturnal waking events as proxy: no spontaneous waking = +1, one brief waking = 0, multiple wakings or 3am arousal = -1.

3. Sleep regularity (prior 7 days). Sleep onset within ±30 minutes of target every night for 7 days = +1, within ±30 minutes for 5-6 of 7 nights = 0, sleep onset varying by >60 minutes on more than 2 nights = -1.

Readiness score interpretation:
- +3: Optimal. Highest-priority WILD night. Deploy advanced techniques. Use WBTB + MILD/SSILD/WILD combined protocol.
- +1 to +2: Ready. Standard WILD practice. Expect above-average performance.
- 0: Marginal. Basic WILD attempt acceptable. Reduce cognitive load — use simple MILD rather than full WILD.
- -1 to -3: Not ready. Tonight is a recovery night. No active WILD attempt. Priority: 8+ hours sleep, no alarm, full recovery protocol (nutrition, no exercise, magnesium, early sleep).

The last point is as important as the first. One of the most common failure patterns in WILD practitioners is attempting the technique on every possible night regardless of system state. This is not discipline — it is signal misinterpretation. A readiness score of -2 means the system is not in a state from which the target state (conscious dream entry) is accessible. Forcing the attempt generates frustration, disrupts the very recovery that would have restored readiness, and creates a negative associative loop around the practice. The system is telling you something. Read it correctly.

Wearable Options

Oura Ring (Gen 3): Currently the most accurate consumer wearable for overnight HRV, sleep staging, and readiness scoring. The ring form factor reduces motion artefact and the sensor position (fingertip) gives high-quality PPG signal. Readiness score algorithm is well-validated against PSG in independent studies. Reports HRV, respiratory rate, skin temperature, and sleep stage breakdown. Recommended for practitioners who want the most detailed readiness data.

WHOOP 4.0: Continuous HRV monitoring (not just overnight), recovery score model, and strain metric. Wrist-based with strong PPG signal from the underside placement. Strong for tracking training load vs. recovery balance. Recovery score and sleep performance metrics are well-designed. No display (data to phone only) — relevant for practitioners who want no light stimulus at night.

Garmin (Fenix, Epix, Forerunner 955+): Body Battery metric is a simplified HRV-derived readiness score. HRV status tracking (7-day baseline comparison) is available in the health snapshot feature. Less granular than Oura or WHOOP but more accessible as a combined fitness-sleep tracker. Good for practitioners already using Garmin for training.

Apple Watch (Series 8+/Ultra): Less reliable for overnight sleep staging than Oura or WHOOP due to wrist placement and battery management during sleep, but the AFib/ECG capability and retrospective HRV data from Health app are useful supplementary data. Not recommended as a primary readiness tracking tool.

Important caveat: All consumer wearables use photoplethysmography (PPG) to estimate HRV, not electrocardiography (ECG). PPG-derived HRV has lower accuracy than gold-standard ECG, particularly at high HRV values. For the purposes of readiness tracking, however, trend consistency matters more than absolute accuracy — and for trend tracking, consumer devices are reliable.

When a Low Readiness Score Means: Do This Instead

A readiness score of 0 or below should trigger a specific protocol rather than a default frustrated non-practice:

  • Set no alarm. Sleep to natural waking.
  • Take magnesium glycinate and L-theanine at standard doses.
  • Eat dinner by 7pm (or three hours before sleep). No late snacking.
  • Avoid screens for the final 60 minutes.
  • Use the 4-7-8 breathing protocol (inhale 4 counts, hold 7, exhale 8) for 4 rounds before sleep — this is a direct parasympathetic activation technique that will improve the following morning's HRV.
  • Journal one page of non-judgmental reflection on whatever is occupying your mind — cognitive arousal is a major HRV suppressor, and written externalisation reduces it.

The recovery night is not wasted practice time. It is the practice. Every high-readiness night is built on the recovery nights that preceded it. Practitioners who treat recovery as passive are leaving half their system on the table.

Key Takeaways

  • HRV (RMSSD, 7-day rolling average, normalised to personal baseline) is the most predictive single metric for deep sleep readiness and SWS architecture — it outperforms all subjective sleepiness measures.
  • HRV trends carry the signal; individual night readings are noisy. A rising 7-day trend indicates building physiological readiness; a falling trend indicates accumulated stress load that must be addressed before demanding practice.
  • Your readiness score (HRV + glucose variability + sleep regularity) is a three-variable composite that scores system navigability from -3 to +3; a +3 score is the highest-priority WILD practice night, a -3 score is a mandatory recovery night.
  • Consumer wearables (Oura Gen 3 preferred) provide sufficient trend accuracy for readiness tracking; their PPG-based HRV estimates are less accurate than ECG for absolute values but reliable for the inter-night trends that matter most.
  • The most important decision your readiness score enables is the binary practice/recovery call: attempting WILD on a low-readiness night is not discipline — it is misuse of the system's signalling, and it should be replaced with the recovery protocol explicitly.

Practice Assignment

Set up your readiness tracking system this week. Create a simple daily log (spreadsheet or notes app) with three columns: HRV reading, nocturnal waking events (yes/no/time), and sleep consistency flag (on-time/late). Score each variable (+1/0/-1) and sum to your daily readiness score. Run this for thirty days. At the end of thirty days, correlate your daily readiness score with your WILD practice outcomes. The correlation is typically strong enough to see clearly by day twenty. Use this data to identify your personal readiness threshold — the minimum score at which your WILD attempts succeed — and make it a hard rule: below that threshold, it is a recovery night.
