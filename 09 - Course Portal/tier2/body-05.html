BODY Body 5

Anti-Inflammatory Nutrition

Video lesson Coming soon — check back after launch
~14 minutes

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the mechanism by which chronic low-grade inflammation elevates baseline cortisol and suppresses REM sleep depth.
  • Identify the principal dietary drivers of inflammatory tone (refined carbohydrates, trans fats, excess omega-6).
  • Describe the anti-inflammatory dietary pattern (omega-3, polyphenols, reduced refined carbohydrates) and its evidence base for sleep quality.
  • Understand the Mediterranean diet research in the context of sleep and cognitive performance.
  • Understand how chronic inflammation functions as a persistent source of physiological disruption that consumes regulatory bandwidth and degrades sleep architecture.

Lesson Content

Inflammation is your immune system's response to threat. In the short term — responding to infection, healing a wound, clearing cellular debris — it is an essential and life-preserving process. In the long term, when it runs at a low background level without a clear threat to resolve, it is a systemic noise source. Chronic low-grade inflammation is not a disease category in most clinical classifications, but it is a pervasive physiological state in modern populations — driven primarily by diet — and its effects on sleep architecture are both well-documented and underappreciated.

The Inflammation-Cortisol-REM Pathway

The pathway runs as follows. Pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-alpha) act on the hypothalamus and pituitary, activating the HPA axis. HPA activation increases cortisol secretion. Cortisol is not inherently sleep-disruptive — its diurnal rhythm (high in the morning, low at night) is actually coordinated with sleep architecture. The problem arises when baseline cortisol is chronically elevated, flattening the diurnal curve and maintaining adrenergic and metabolic activation into the sleep period.

Specifically, elevated evening cortisol: delays sleep onset by maintaining wakefulness through corticotropin pathways; suppresses slow-wave sleep in the early part of the night by reducing growth hormone release (GH is released in the first SWS bout and is inhibited by cortisol); and fragments REM by increasing arousal threshold sensitivity in the second half of the night.

Additionally, pro-inflammatory cytokines (particularly IL-1β and TNF-alpha) directly modulate sleep architecture via independent central nervous system mechanisms — they cross the blood-brain barrier and act on sleep-regulatory regions including the anterior hypothalamus and brainstem. At moderate levels, IL-1β actually promotes NREM sleep (part of the immune system's resource-allocation strategy during infection). At chronically elevated low levels, the signal becomes noise — it disrupts the precise cycling between sleep stages rather than deepening any particular stage.

Dietary Drivers of Inflammation

Refined carbohydrates and added sugar produce postprandial glucose spikes that activate the NF-κB inflammatory signalling pathway. AGEs (advanced glycation end-products) form when excess glucose binds to proteins, and AGEs directly stimulate inflammatory cytokine production.

Trans fatty acids (partially hydrogenated oils, present in many processed foods) are among the most potent dietary pro-inflammatory agents. They incorporate into cell membranes, alter membrane fluidity, and increase production of arachidonic acid metabolites (pro-inflammatory eicosanoids).

Omega-6 to omega-3 imbalance is the dominant dietary inflammation driver in modern diets. The ideal omega-6:omega-3 ratio is approximately 4:1. The modern Western diet typically runs at 15:1 to 20:1, primarily due to the ubiquity of seed oils (sunflower, safflower, corn, soybean) as cooking oils and food ingredients. Excess omega-6 (linoleic acid) is converted to arachidonic acid, the precursor to pro-inflammatory prostaglandins and leukotrienes.

Ultra-processed foods combine multiple inflammatory drivers: refined carbohydrates, seed oils, emulsifiers, and food additives. Their consumption is consistently the strongest dietary predictor of inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6) in epidemiological data.

The Anti-Inflammatory Dietary Pattern

Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA, found in fatty fish: salmon, mackerel, sardines, herring; and in concentrated algae-based supplements for non-fish consumers) are direct precursors to anti-inflammatory resolvins and protectins — signalling molecules that actively resolve inflammation rather than simply inhibiting it. The research on omega-3 and sleep is robust: EPA/DHA supplementation (1-3g/day) reduces inflammatory markers, improves sleep onset latency, and increases REM duration in controlled trials.

Polyphenols are plant-derived compounds found in berries, dark chocolate (70%+ cocoa), olive oil, green tea, turmeric (curcumin), and colourful vegetables. They inhibit NF-κB signalling, reduce oxidative stress, and support the gut microbiome (many polyphenols are prebiotic). Curcumin specifically has been shown to reduce IL-6 and TNF-alpha in RCTs; resveratrol (found in grapes and berries) activates SIRT1 pathways associated with circadian rhythm regulation.

Reduced refined carbohydrates — not zero carbohydrates, but replacing refined sources with whole grain, legume, and vegetable carbohydrates — is among the most effective single dietary changes for reducing postprandial glycaemic spiking and NF-κB activation.

Mediterranean Diet and Sleep Research

The Mediterranean dietary pattern — high in vegetables, legumes, olive oil, fatty fish, whole grains, and nuts; low in processed meats and refined carbohydrates — is the single most studied dietary pattern for both cardiovascular health and cognitive performance. It is increasingly studied specifically for sleep.

A 2017 cohort study (PREDIMED-Plus) found that adherence to the Mediterranean diet was associated with significantly better sleep duration, reduced insomnia symptoms, and higher subjective sleep quality. The 2019 PREDIMED data confirmed associations between olive oil intake and reduced sleep disorders. Mechanistically, the pattern reduces CRP and IL-6, improves the omega-6:omega-3 ratio, and increases fibre intake — hitting all three primary anti-inflammatory levers simultaneously.

For WILD practitioners, the practical implication is not that you need to move to Crete. It is that the Mediterranean pattern provides a practical framework: build each meal around vegetables and legumes as the base, use olive oil as the primary fat, include fatty fish two to three times per week, minimise refined carbohydrates, and treat processed food as the exception rather than the default.

Inflammation as a Persistent Disruption Source

Think of physiological readiness as the system's ability to transition reliably between states and maintain stability within them. Inputs that consume that regulatory bandwidth without advancing any useful function are sources of interference. Chronic low-grade inflammation is precisely this: it is a background signal activating immune and HPA pathways continuously, keeping the nervous system in a state of chronic sub-threshold alert, consuming metabolic and regulatory resources that should be available for high-performance sleep architecture.

A useful analogy: a signal processor running background interference at 30% of its bandwidth can still function, but its signal resolution is degraded. Inflammatory noise occupies regulatory bandwidth continuously — HPA reactivity is heightened, autonomic flexibility is reduced (visible in HRV suppression), and the precise neurochemical cycling that produces deep, architecturally clean sleep is blunted. Reducing inflammation is foundational recovery work: removing the interference before attempting to build on top of the system.

Key Takeaways

  • Chronic low-grade inflammation activates the HPA axis via cytokine signalling, elevating baseline cortisol, suppressing SWS, delaying sleep onset, and fragmenting REM in the second half of the night.
  • The primary dietary drivers of systemic inflammation are refined carbohydrates, trans fats, excess omega-6 seed oils, and ultra-processed food additives — all addressable through targeted dietary adjustment.
  • The anti-inflammatory dietary pattern centres on omega-3 fatty acids (1-3g EPA/DHA daily), polyphenol-rich plants, and replacement of refined carbohydrates with whole food sources.
  • The Mediterranean dietary pattern provides the most evidence-based practical framework for achieving anti-inflammatory dietary composition while remaining nutritionally complete and sustainable.
  • Chronic inflammation is a persistent source of physiological disruption: it consumes regulatory bandwidth via chronic HPA activation, degrades HRV, and blunts the precise neurochemical conditions that REM sleep requires.

Practice Assignment

Conduct a two-week anti-inflammatory dietary trial. The three non-negotiable changes: (1) replace all cooking oils with extra-virgin olive oil and add fatty fish twice per week (or begin omega-3 supplementation at 2g EPA/DHA daily); (2) eliminate all ultra-processed foods for fourteen days — this means reading ingredient labels and avoiding anything containing partially hydrogenated oils, carboxymethylcellulose, or more than five unrecognisable ingredients; (3) add one polyphenol-rich food to each meal (berries at breakfast, a large serving of colourful vegetables at lunch, dark chocolate or green tea post-dinner). Track HRV daily if you have a wearable — anti-inflammatory dietary changes typically produce measurable HRV improvement within seven to ten days.
