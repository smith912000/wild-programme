The Problem With Optimising in Silos

You've spent the Body track learning how the physical system works. You've learned what tryptophan does upstream of melatonin, how circadian eating stabilises REM timing, why glucose volatility fragments your WILD window, how the gut-brain axis regulates neurotransmitter availability, what inflammation costs you in autonomic bandwidth, when exercise builds readiness versus when it destroys it, which foundational supplements earn their place, and how to read HRV as a real-time measure of system navigability.

You've also worked through the Soul track. You've developed the attentional precision to hold the WILD thread through the hypnagogic transition. You've learned to stabilise lucid state, extend duration, and work with dream architecture deliberately. You've practised DEILD chains, SSILD cycling, and reality-testing systems that run on automatic.

Here's the gap most practitioners fall into: they treat these two tracks as parallel systems that happen to share a sleep window. They do the Body work on nutrition days and the Soul work on practice nights. The two remain conceptually separate.

That separation is the mistake. Because the Body track is not support infrastructure for the Soul track. The Body track is the amplifier. The physical variables you've been optimising are the conditions under which the conscious techniques you've learned either work or fail. Get the amplifier wrong and the technique is transmitting into noise. Get the amplifier right and the technique runs cleanly on a high signal-to-noise system that almost gets out of its own way.

This module is about closing that gap. Mechanistically. Practically. With a protocol you can run starting this week.

How Each Body Variable Directly Enables Advanced WILD Practice

Let's go system by system. Not abstractly — mechanistically. For each body variable, the question is the same: what specifically happens to your WILD practice when this variable is dialled in versus disordered?

Sleep-Nutrition Axis (B1)

When tryptophan conversion is optimised — mixed meal three to four hours before bed, alcohol absent, high-glycaemic foods out of the late window — melatonin synthesis peaks cleanly over sleep initiation. What this does for WILD practice: sleep onset is faster, the first deep SWS block is consolidated rather than fragmented, and the REM rebound that follows is more architecturally intact. That means your WBTB window hits real extended REM rather than transitional, shallow REM. Hypnagogic access is deeper and more stable because the underlying REM generation is running at full amplitude. Micro-arousals — the tiny disruptions that knock you out of the WILD thread just as entry is occurring — decrease sharply when REM is architecturally intact rather than fragmented.

The failure mode: a disordered nutrition night (alcohol, late high-glycaemic food, large meal volume) produces fragmented REM, which feels like repeated near-entries that collapse. Practitioners often blame their technique. The technique is fine. The substrate it's running on is broken.

Circadian Eating (B2)

Late eating desynchronises peripheral clocks from the master circadian clock. That desynchrony displaces REM propensity — the window during which your brain is primed to generate REM — by up to ninety minutes. For WILD practice, this means your WBTB alarm hits either too early (before peak REM) or, if sleep duration is fixed, the REM-rich window has been compressed out of your available sleep time entirely.

When circadian eating is consistent — a nine-to-ten hour feeding window closing three or more hours before sleep onset — peripheral clocks align with your SCN, melatonin onset sharpens, and your personal REM propensity window lands exactly where your WBTB timing expects it. Advanced techniques that require sustained REM contact — extended DEILD chains, deliberate navigation, extended duration work — only become available when the window is correctly positioned and of sufficient duration. Circadian eating alignment is what positions the window correctly.

Blood Sugar Stability (B3)

A glucose trough during the night (typically caused by high-glycaemic eating within ninety minutes of bed) triggers a cortisol release from the adrenal glands. Cortisol is an arousal hormone. It elevates sympathetic tone. It fragments sleep at exactly the phase transition from SWS to REM — the early hours of the second half of the night — which is precisely when the WILD window begins to build.

Stable overnight glucose means: no cortisol spike in the early morning hours, no fragmentation of the SWS-to-REM transition, and — critically — no micro-awakenings during the WILD window itself. Micro-awakenings during WILD entry are often misread as technique failure. Most commonly they are glucose instability events. Stable glucose is what keeps the WILD window quiet enough for maintained conscious entry. Deeper hypnagogic access is a direct consequence: a nervous system not managing a glucose emergency has its full resolution available for the subtler signals of the hypnagogic state.

Gut-Brain Axis (B4)

Approximately ninety percent of the body's serotonin is synthesised in the gut. Serotonin is the precursor to melatonin, and it is also a primary regulatory neurotransmitter for mood, emotional tone, and the quality of the pre-sleep mental state. A gut microbiome with poor diversity and inadequate prebiotic substrate produces reduced serotonin, blunted GABA signalling, and elevated systemic inflammation — all of which degrade sleep quality and raise the difficulty threshold for quiet pre-sleep attention.

When gut health is supported — diverse plant food intake, prebiotic fibre, fermented foods — the neurotransmitter signalling environment improves. The practical effect on WILD practice: pre-sleep mental quietude is easier to access, the transition into hypnagogia is smoother, and the quality of the REM-consciousness state is richer. Advanced practitioners consistently report that their best WILD experiences cluster in periods when gut health is good. This is not coincidence — it is neurotransmitter substrate availability.

Anti-Inflammatory Nutrition (B5)

Chronic low-grade inflammation — from ultra-processed foods, seed oils, poor sleep, chronic stress — maintains a persistent background activation of the HPA axis. Elevated basal cortisol at low levels, around the clock, consumes autonomic regulatory bandwidth. The measurable effect is HRV suppression. The practical effect on WILD practice is two-fold: first, sleep architecture is blunted (REM episodes are shorter and shallower), and second, the capacity for deep parasympathetic relaxation — which is a prerequisite for holding the conscious WILD thread through sleep onset — is reduced.

When inflammation is low, HRV is higher, REM is deeper and longer, and the body's capacity for deep physical relaxation while maintaining conscious awareness is substantially greater. This is not subtle. Practitioners who run an anti-inflammatory dietary protocol for two to four weeks and track WILD outcomes consistently report improvements in entry rate and in the quality of the lucid state itself. Lower inflammation means more system bandwidth available for exactly the kind of precise, deliberate state navigation that advanced WILD requires.

Exercise Timing (B6)

Morning exercise — particularly aerobic work outdoors — amplifies the cortisol awakening response, sharpens circadian phase, and produces an adenosine debt that drives deeper SWS in the following night. Deeper SWS in the first half of the night creates a stronger REM rebound in the second half. That stronger REM rebound is what produces the extended, high-quality WILD windows that advanced techniques require.

Evening vigorous exercise within three to four hours of sleep onset delays sleep onset, elevates pre-sleep core body temperature, and can suppress REM duration in the first two cycles. The timing rule is simple: intense exercise before noon maximises WILD quality that night; intense exercise after 6 PM degrades it. The body, properly exercised at the right time, essentially primes its own optimal sleep architecture.

Foundational Supplements (B7)

Magnesium glycinate supports NMDA receptor function and parasympathetic tone, both of which reduce sleep onset latency and increase deep SWS quality. The direct WILD application: faster sleep onset means you hit your first SWS block earlier, the SWS block consolidates more deeply, and the REM rebound is stronger. L-theanine taken at WBTB return (not at initial sleep) raises alpha wave activity — the state most associated with relaxed, open awareness — which lowers the attentional threshold for conscious entry. Melatonin at low doses (0.3mg), when used for circadian calibration rather than sedation, sharpens sleep onset timing and reduces the night-to-night variability in WBTB window accuracy.

The combined supplement stack does not replace technique. It improves the physiological conditions in which technique operates — reducing the entry difficulty and making the practice more consistent across varying life circumstances.

HRV and Readiness Score (B8)

HRV is the unifying signal. It integrates information from every other body variable — nutrition, glucose, inflammation, exercise load, sleep regularity — into a single autonomic nervous system output. A high HRV (above your personal 7-day baseline) reflects a system that is parasympathetically dominant, metabolically coherent, and neurochemically primed for deep sleep architecture. A low HRV reflects accumulated systemic stress load, regardless of its source.

For WILD practice, HRV is the most direct predictive signal available. On high-HRV nights, every other body variable is likely aligned: melatonin synthesis is clean, glucose is stable, inflammation is low, cortisol is quiet. These are the nights when technique works with minimal friction — when entry happens smoothly, stabilisation holds, and duration extends. On low-HRV nights, attempting advanced techniques is fighting upstream against the system's own signalling. The practice/recovery binary is the most important daily decision in a serious WILD protocol.

The Weekly Rhythm: Stacking Body and Soul Practices

The goal is not to run Body practices on some days and Soul practices on others. The goal is to stack them — to design a weekly rhythm in which the Body inputs are creating the conditions, and the Soul practice is executing in those conditions.

Here is how that stacking works in practice.

The architecture of a WILD week:

A realistic weekly WILD practice schedule targets two to three WBTB attempts per week. Not every night — sleep debt accumulates, HRV drops, and nightly attempts reduce overall sleep quality in ways that compound across the week. Two to three attempts per week, with recovery nights between, is the highest-yield protocol for most practitioners.

Around those two to three practice nights, the Body practices create the conditions. The logic:

Two days before a planned WILD night: This is a high-value Body compliance day. Nutrition is clean — tryptophan-containing dinner, no alcohol, no late high-glycaemic food. Exercise is morning-anchored. Circadian eating window closes by 6-7 PM. You are building the platform two days out because sleep architecture from two nights prior influences HRV the following morning — and HRV the morning before your WILD night determines whether the attempt proceeds.

One day before a planned WILD night: Check HRV. If it is above baseline, the attempt is confirmed. If it is below baseline by more than 5%, consider shifting the attempt forward one day. This is not timidity — this is system literacy. No late eating, no alcohol, no vigorous evening exercise. Light walking or yoga only after 5 PM. Prebiotic-rich dinner, magnesium glycinate at bedtime. Pre-sleep journal entry: one page, clearing cognitive noise so the pre-sleep mind is quiet.

The WILD night itself: Execute your personalised WBTB window. L-theanine at WBTB return (100mg). Full Soul track technique: SSILD cycling, DEILD chain readiness, or your primary advanced induction method. Body is in conditions — technique has maximum runway.

The day after: Recovery. No alarm. Sleep until natural waking. Morning HRV reading. Light food, anti-inflammatory emphasis. Light outdoor walk. Note any dream content from the previous night — this data informs technique adjustments.

Recovery nights (non-WILD nights): Maintain Body practices consistently. This is important. Body practices only produce their full effect when they are consistent, not when they are switched on the night before practice and switched off on recovery nights. Circadian eating is a consistency signal; it works when it is habitual. Anti-inflammatory nutrition is cumulative; it requires a week-plus of consistent input before HRV begins to rise. The recovery nights are when you build the readiness that the practice nights draw from.

Weekly check-in: Sunday or Monday morning, review your readiness data from the past week. HRV trend: rising, flat, or falling? Sleep regularity: consistent or variable? Glucose proxy (quality of pre-bed nutrition and sleep feel): stable or disordered? Adjust the following week's practice schedule based on what the trend is telling you. If HRV trended down across the week, shift one of the three planned practice nights to recovery. If HRV trended up across the week, all three nights proceed.

The Soul practices in the weekly rhythm:

Dream journal — daily, every morning within five minutes of waking. This is not optional and it is not body practice or soul practice — it is the foundational data-collection discipline that makes everything else interpretable. Without consistent dream journalling, you cannot track your WILD quality trajectory accurately.

Reality testing — five to ten times daily, triggered. This is maintenance, not peak practice. It keeps the metacognitive reflex active so that it runs automatically at WILD entry without deliberate effort.

Visualisation and pre-sleep attention practice — on WILD nights only, during the WBTB window before returning to sleep. This is the technical execution of the Soul track. It runs on the Body platform you have built.

Advanced technique variation — introduce one new advanced technique per month, test it across four practice attempts, assess against your baseline WILD entry rate. Do not layer multiple new techniques simultaneously; the readiness data becomes uninterpretable.

When to Attempt Advanced Techniques vs When the Body Score Says Recover

This is the most practically important decision point in the combined protocol, and it is the one most frequently mishandled.

The default assumption most practitioners carry is that more practice equals more progress. This is true on high-readiness nights. It is false on low-readiness nights. On a low-readiness night, attempting WILD is not neutral — it is actively counterproductive. It consumes sleep time in a fragmented system, generates frustration that creates negative associative patterns around the practice, and disrupts the very recovery that would have restored readiness for the following night.

The readiness threshold for advanced Soul track techniques: A readiness score of +2 or +3 is the green window for advanced practice — extended DEILD chains, deliberate narrative construction, extended duration work, complex reality integration exercises. All three body variables are positive; the system has full navigability.

A readiness score of +1 is an amber window. Basic WBTB with primary induction technique (SSILD or your established method). Skip advanced variations. Standard practice, standard expectations.

A readiness score of 0 is a marginal amber. If you have strong momentum — several consecutive successful nights, high motivation, no accumulated sleep debt — attempt basic WBTB only. If any of those conditions are absent, convert to a recovery night.

A readiness score of -1, -2, or -3 is a recovery night. No WBTB. Full sleep. Recovery protocol (4-7-8 breathing, one-page journal, anti-inflammatory food emphasis the following day). This is not giving up. This is precision. Your next practice night will be higher readiness because of the recovery night that preceded it.

The minimum compliance threshold for Body track before attempting advanced Soul track:

  • Nutrition compliance (no alcohol, no late high-glycaemic food, tryptophan-containing dinner at correct timing): minimum 5 of 7 days in the preceding week.
  • Circadian eating window consistency (closing 3+ hours before sleep): minimum 5 of 7 days.
  • Exercise timing (morning-anchored vigorous exercise, no vigorous exercise after 6 PM on practice days): minimum 3 of 5 training days.
  • Foundational supplement stack: magnesium glycinate nightly, L-theanine at WBTB return on practice nights.
  • HRV above personal 7-day baseline on the morning of the practice night.

If fewer than four of these five conditions are met, the readiness threshold for advanced Soul track techniques has not been reached. Run basic WBTB at most. Do not attempt extended or complex techniques.

This threshold may feel conservative. It is not. It is calibrated against the actual physiological conditions under which advanced WILD techniques produce reliable results. Practitioners who ignore the threshold report high variability and a sense that the practice is inconsistent or unpredictable. Practitioners who honour it report that advanced techniques feel almost inevitable on high-compliance nights — that the practice runs with a quality of ease that is qualitatively different from forced attempts.

The Readiness Threshold Concept

A readiness threshold is the minimum system state from which a target state is reliably accessible. Below the threshold, the target state is theoretically possible but practically unreliable — you might succeed, but you cannot predict when, and each failure reinforces a sense of randomness around the practice. Above the threshold, the target state is consistently accessible — success rate rises above 50% per attempt, and the quality of the state itself is higher when it occurs.

Your personal readiness threshold is not fixed. It is determined by your current fitness level, your Body track compliance consistency, and the accumulated skill level of your Soul track practice. As Body track compliance becomes habitual and Soul track skill deepens, the minimum readiness required for a successful attempt decreases. You become a more capable practitioner in the full sense: both more physically prepared on average nights, and more technically skilled at executing in less-than-ideal conditions.

Track your readiness score against your WILD outcomes over thirty days. By day twenty, the correlation will be visible. Identify the score level at which your personal success rate crosses 50%. That number is your personal readiness threshold. Make it a hard rule. It is more reliable than intuition and more honest than optimism.

This challenge is designed to generate thirty days of integrated data — the minimum needed to identify your personal readiness threshold, observe the Body-Soul correlation in your own results, and establish the combined protocol as a stable habit.

What you will need:
- Your existing sleep log (from Tier 1 and/or Tier 2 practice)
- HRV wearable or basic morning HRV app (Oura, WHOOP, Garmin, or free app with camera-based HRV)
- A simple daily log — five data points per day: HRV reading, sleep regularity score (+1/0/-1), nocturnal waking events (proxy for glucose stability, +1 = no waking, 0 = one waking, -1 = two or more), daily readiness score sum, and one-line practice outcome note

Week 1 — Baseline and Foundation

Goal: Establish your current baseline with no deliberate Body track changes. Observe what your readiness score actually looks like before optimisation. Run your standard WILD practice on two nights, track outcomes honestly.

Daily actions:
- Log all five data points each morning within fifteen minutes of waking.
- Dream journal within five minutes of waking, every morning.
- Reality-test five to ten times daily using your established trigger system.
- No changes to nutrition, exercise, or supplements yet — this is your baseline week.

Week 1 WILD nights: Two attempts, on whichever nights your current readiness is highest. Record: entry success (yes/no), entry speed (minutes from WBTB return to entry), duration (minutes of lucid state), quality (1-5, subjective), and one technique note.

Week 1 reflection (Sunday evening): Review your five-day readiness scores. What is your current average? What is the highest night you had? What was your WILD success rate on the two attempt nights? This is your pre-optimisation baseline.

Week 2 — Body Track Activation

Goal: Implement full Body track compliance and observe HRV, sleep architecture, and WILD quality changes relative to your Week 1 baseline.

Daily actions (all seven days):
- Tryptophan-containing dinner with moderate complex carbs, eaten 3-4 hours before sleep onset.
- Eating window closed 3+ hours before bed, every day.
- No alcohol, no high-GI food within 90 minutes of bed.
- Morning exercise (minimum 20 minutes aerobic, outdoors if possible) on at least four of seven days.
- No vigorous exercise after 6 PM.
- Magnesium glycinate 200mg at bedtime, every night.
- Anti-inflammatory food emphasis: no ultra-processed food, olive oil for cooking, fatty fish or omega-3 supplement, one polyphenol-rich food per meal.
- Pre-sleep one-page journal on all seven nights.
- Log all five data points each morning.

Week 2 WILD nights: Three attempts — you have now earned the additional night through full Body compliance. On each practice night: L-theanine 100mg at WBTB return. Use your primary established induction technique (not new advanced variations — this week is about observing Body track impact on existing technique performance, not introducing technique variables).

Week 2 reflection: Compare your Week 2 average readiness score to Week 1 average. Has HRV begun rising? Compare WILD success rate and quality to Week 1. Document what changed and what stayed the same.

Week 3 — Advanced Technique Integration

Goal: On high-readiness nights (score +2 or +3), introduce one advanced technique you have not yet used consistently. Observe whether readiness score predicts which nights the advanced technique succeeds.

Daily actions: All Week 2 Body practices continue unchanged.

Advanced technique selection (choose one):
- Extended DEILD chain (target three consecutive entries from a single WBTB)
- Deliberate narrative construction (set a specific intention before entry and observe whether the dream content reflects it)
- Extended duration work (target five minutes of maintained lucidity per entry rather than standard duration)

Week 3 WILD nights: Three attempts. On +2 or +3 readiness nights, attempt the selected advanced technique. On +1 readiness nights, run standard practice only.

Week 3 prediction protocol: Before each attempt night, write a one-line prediction: "My readiness score is [X]. I predict this will be a [successful/partial/failed] advanced technique attempt." After the attempt, compare prediction to outcome. By the end of Week 3, you will be able to see whether your readiness score is predicting your outcomes accurately. For most practitioners, it is.

Week 3 reflection: How often did the readiness score predict the outcome correctly? Where did it miss, and why? What did you notice about the quality difference between +1, +2, and +3 readiness nights?

Week 4 — Personal Protocol Lock

Goal: Synthesise your thirty days of data into your personal integrated protocol. Identify your personal readiness threshold. Build the Week 5 onward protocol that you will run indefinitely.

Daily actions: All Body practices continue. This week, your focus is on analysis alongside practice.

Readiness threshold analysis (do this on Day 25 of the challenge):
- List all WILD attempt nights from the past three weeks.
- Against each, note: readiness score, attempt type (basic/advanced), success (yes/partial/no).
- Find the readiness score at which your success rate (successful or partial) crosses 50%. This is your personal readiness threshold.

Week 4 WILD nights: Three attempts, using your established weekly rhythm. Run whichever technique the readiness score authorises.

Personal Protocol Document (complete by Day 28):
Build a one-page personal protocol that specifies:
1. Your WBTB window (personalised from Tier 2 Lesson 1 timing calculation).
2. Your pre-bed nutrition rules (timed to your specific sleep schedule).
3. Your exercise anchor (specific time and format that works for your weekly structure).
4. Your supplement stack (current, with doses).
5. Your readiness threshold (the score number you identified in the analysis above).
6. Your practice/recovery decision rule: "If morning readiness score is [X] or above, tonight is a WILD night. If below, tonight is a recovery night."
7. Your advanced technique availability threshold: "Advanced techniques only on readiness score of [X+1] or above."
8. Your weekly WILD schedule: which days, how many attempts, how many recovery nights.

Final Week 4 reflection: At thirty days, you have a complete integrated protocol and thirty days of outcome data to validate it. The practice is no longer experimental. It is calibrated. It is yours.

Now — the one that I think is most underappreciated: circadian eating alignment and what it does to the REM propensity window.

Most practitioners spend significant time personalising their WBTB timing. Good. That's the right focus. But the timing calculation assumes your REM propensity window is where you think it is. And for practitioners who are eating late — regularly closing the eating window within two hours of bed — the circadian phase is displaced. Peripheral clocks are signalling late-phase. The REM propensity window shifts with them. So you have personalised your WBTB to what your theoretical REM window should be, but your actual REM window is sitting sixty to ninety minutes later than that. You're arriving early to a window that hasn't opened yet.

The fix is not to change your WBTB timing. The fix is to align your circadian clocks so that your REM window is where your timing expects it to be. And that means closing your eating window consistently — not perfectly, but consistently — three or more hours before sleep onset. Give it two weeks and the window sharpens. Give it four weeks and the correlation between your WBTB timing and actual REM contact becomes tight enough to feel reliable for the first time.

Okay — let me talk about the weekly structure, because this is where the integration becomes practical.

The mistake I see practitioners make is treating body practices as switches. They do the nutrition right on the night before practice and then drop it on recovery nights. That's not how any of these systems work. Melatonin synthesis is a daily process, not a switch you flip. HRV is a trend, not a night-by-night reading. Anti-inflammatory nutrition takes two weeks to show measurable HRV improvement. You cannot build a high-readiness system by being compliant one day in three.

The body practices are the foundation. They run continuously. The practice windows are what you do in the conditions those practices create.

So here's the weekly architecture that I've found works.

Two to three WBTB attempts per week. Not more. Sleep debt is a real HRV suppressor — attempting every night progressively reduces readiness across the week, and by day five or six, you're attempting practice in a system that is measurably compromised. Two to three attempts per week, with genuine recovery nights between, produces better results than five or six attempts with accumulated debt.

Around those practice nights, the body work is consistent all week. Eating window timing doesn't change between practice nights and recovery nights. Anti-inflammatory food doesn't switch off on non-practice days. Exercise timing stays morning-anchored regardless. The only thing that changes on practice nights is the WBTB alarm and the L-theanine at WBTB return.

Two days before a planned WILD night, your nutrition is particularly clean — that's when it matters most for HRV on the morning of the practice night. One day before, check your HRV trend. If it's moving in the right direction, confirm the attempt. If it's dropped significantly below baseline, consider shifting the attempt forward by a day and running a recovery night instead.

That single decision — confirm or shift — is the most important decision point in the protocol. And it requires having the data to make it. Which is why the tracking log is non-negotiable. You cannot make a system-informed decision without system data.

Now let me address the readiness threshold directly, because this is the concept I want you to leave this module with.

Your readiness threshold is the minimum system state from which conscious dream entry is reliably accessible. Not possible — reliable. Below the threshold, it might happen. But you can't count on it, and the failure rate erodes motivation in ways that compound over time. Above the threshold, the success rate crosses fifty percent per attempt. The technique almost runs itself on a well-prepared system.

What I want you to do with the 4-week integration challenge is find your personal threshold. Your body is not the same as mine. Your baseline HRV is different. Your glucose sensitivity is different. The specific combination of variables that puts you in a high-readiness state is individual. The only way to know it precisely is thirty days of correlated data.

Here's the prediction protocol that accelerates the learning. Before each WILD attempt night, write one line: "My readiness score is [X]. I predict this will be a [successful / partial / failed] entry night." After the attempt, compare prediction to outcome. Within two weeks, you will start getting the predictions right most of the time. When you can reliably predict your outcomes from your readiness score, you have identified your personal threshold. Make it a hard rule from that point forward.

The reason the hard rule matters: practitioners who treat the threshold as a soft guideline — who attempt practice when the score is below threshold "just to try" — consistently report that their practice feels unpredictable and inconsistent. Practitioners who treat it as a hard rule report the opposite. The practice becomes reliable, which changes the psychological relationship with it. It stops feeling like something that sometimes works and sometimes doesn't. It starts feeling like a system with known inputs and predictable outputs.

That psychological shift is actually significant for practice quality, not just for motivation. When you enter a WILD attempt knowing your system is prepared, the pre-sleep attention is cleaner. The practice runs better because you're not fighting doubt at the point of entry.

Advanced techniques — extended DEILD chains, deliberate narrative construction, extended duration targets — are not available below threshold. That's not a rule I invented. It's an empirical observation: on readiness nights below +2, advanced techniques fail at a rate high enough that attempting them is counterproductive. On +2 and +3 nights, they succeed at a rate high enough that they reliably extend your capability. Use the score to gate the technique level. Basic practice on amber nights, advanced practice on green nights, recovery protocol on red nights.
