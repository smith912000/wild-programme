# TIER 3 LIVE SESSION FRAMEWORK
## The WILD Programme — Master Level
### Live Programme Design & Hosting Guide

---

**Price point:** £200 (Tier 3 total)
**Live formats:** Monthly Q&A (x12) | Quarterly Workshop (x4) | Annual 3-Day Virtual Retreat (x1)
**Platform prerequisites:** Zoom (or equivalent) + course platform replay hosting

## PART ONE: MONTHLY Q&A SESSIONS
### 12 Sessions Per Year | 60 Minutes Each

---

### PURPOSE AND POSITIONING

The Monthly Q&A is the connective tissue of the Tier 3 experience. Written content teaches the framework. Video lessons demonstrate the techniques. The live call is where participants translate both into their actual practice — with real questions, real obstacles, and real-time feedback.

Each session follows a fixed format so participants know exactly what to expect and can prepare accordingly. The 12-month rotation maps the Q&A themes directly to the Body-Mind-Soul framework of the written course, ensuring that live discussion deepens whatever area of the curriculum is most active for participants at that point in the year.

---

### 12-MONTH THEME ROTATION

The rotation begins in the month the cohort starts. For a January cohort, the mapping below applies. For rolling enrolments, participants join the live rotation at whatever month is current and cycle through the full 12. All replays are available from day one.

| Month | Theme | Course Connection | Primary Focus |
|-------|-------|-------------------|---------------|
| 1 | Body Track Fundamentals | Body 1-2: Neuroperformance nutrition, HRV baseline | Establishing physiological inputs — diet, sleep architecture, HRV tracking setup |
| 2 | Circadian Engineering | Body 3-4: Circadian rhythm optimisation, light exposure | Timing protocols, WBTB scheduling, shift work adaptations |
| 3 | Cholinergic Stack Precision | Body 5-6: Advanced supplementation, galantamine protocols | Dosing, cycling, stacking strategies, individual variation |
| 4 | Attention Architecture | Mind 1-2: Meta-awareness, attention training | Induction technique refinement, MILD/WILD hybrid protocols |
| 5 | Emotional Regulation and REM | Mind 3-4: Stress, HRV, nervous system state | Pre-sleep nervous system priming, anxiety management, body scan protocols |
| 6 | Dream Engineering | Mind 5-6: Intention setting, dream control, reality testing | Moving from induction to skill inside the dream state |
| 7 | Integration Mid-Point Review | All tracks: 6-month audit | Practitioner self-assessment, data review, protocol adjustment |
| 8 | Advanced Induction Systems | Soul 1-2: Extended WILD, DEILD chains, SSILD | Technique combinations, hypnagogic exploitation, transition mapping |
| 9 | Neuroscience Deepdive | Soul 3-4: Neural correlates, consciousness research | Applying theory to practice — what the research means for individual protocol design |
| 10 | Performance Application | Soul 5: Cognitive transfer, waking performance metrics | Using lucid dream content for problem-solving, skill rehearsal, creative work |
| 11 | System Optimisation | All tracks: Annual refinement | Identifying weakest link in the system, targeted protocol adjustment |
| 12 | Annual Review and Year Ahead | Full curriculum synthesis | Data review, protocol documentation, goal-setting for the next cycle |

---

### SESSION STRUCTURE (60 MINUTES)

**10 minutes — Month's Theme Recap**

Open with a tight, structured summary of the current month's focus. This is not a re-delivery of the written lesson. It is a distillation of the 2-3 mechanisms that matter most for this month, followed by the practical implication participants should be applying right now.

Format:
- 90-second framing: what this month's theme is and why it matters at this stage of practice
- 5-minute mechanism walkthrough: the core neuroscience or physiological principle driving the theme (slides optional — keep it focused)
- 2-minute practical anchor: the one thing participants should be doing this month, clearly stated

**45 minutes — Live Q&A**

The primary value delivery. Structured as follows:

- Pre-submitted questions first (20-25 minutes): these have been reviewed and selected in advance (see Question Collection Process below). Work through 4-6 questions in depth. If a question is technical, answer it fully — Tier 3 participants are advanced practitioners who can handle detail.
- Open floor questions (15-20 minutes): unmute participants or use the chat/raise hand function. Take 3-5 live questions. Prioritise questions that benefit the group over highly individual edge cases.
- Pattern synthesis (3-5 minutes): before closing the Q&A, briefly identify the common thread running through the questions. This is often the most useful moment — when participants see that their individual struggle is a recognisable pattern.

**5 minutes — Next Month Preview**

Close with a clear setup for the following month:
- Name the next month's theme
- Tell participants what to focus on in the written curriculum before the next call
- Give one concrete preparation task (e.g., "Start tracking HRV daily this week so you have 30 days of data to bring to next month's call")

---

### QUESTION COLLECTION PROCESS

Questions must be submitted at least 48 hours before each session. This allows the host to:
- Select the most widely applicable questions
- Cluster similar questions and address them together
- Research any technical gaps before going live

**Recommended collection method:**

Use a simple Google Form or Typeform with three fields:
1. Your name (optional)
2. Your question (250 words max)
3. How long you have been practicing WILD (for context)

Distribute the form link via the course platform announcement, the Discord/Telegram community, and a dedicated email sent 7 days before the session with a reminder at 48 hours.

**Question selection criteria:**
- Prioritise questions that are specific enough to be answerable but general enough to apply to multiple participants
- Deprioritise questions that require individual diagnosis without data (e.g., "why am I not getting results?" without any tracking data provided)
- Include at least one question from a beginner-level Tier 3 participant per session to maintain accessibility
- Aim for a mix of Body, Mind, and technique questions even if the month's theme is specific

**During the call:**

Read each pre-submitted question aloud before answering it. This ensures participants who didn't submit that question can follow the context. Attribute anonymously by default ("Someone asked...") unless the participant has explicitly given permission to be named.

---

### HOST PREPARATION CHECKLIST

**Seven days before:**
- [ ] Send question collection form to all Tier 3 participants via email and community post
- [ ] Review the current month's written course content to refresh depth on the theme
- [ ] Note any recent developments in the research area (PubMed, preprints) relevant to the month's theme
- [ ] Check whether any common issues have surfaced in the community channels this month — these inform what questions to prioritise

**48 hours before:**
- [ ] Close the question collection form
- [ ] Read all submitted questions
- [ ] Select 5-7 questions for the pre-submitted slot; note which to combine or cluster
- [ ] Prepare brief notes (bullet points only — no scripts) for the theme recap section
- [ ] Test the Zoom link, screen share if using slides, and audio

**Day of session:**
- [ ] Open the room 10 minutes early — participants will join before the start time
- [ ] Have the question list open in front of you
- [ ] Keep a notepad for live questions from the chat
- [ ] Set a timer for each section (10/45/5 split) — it is easy to overrun on Q&A

**Immediately after:**
- [ ] Upload the recording to the course platform replay library within 24 hours (same-day target)
- [ ] Post a 3-5 bullet summary of the key points covered to the community channel
- [ ] Send the next question collection form link to the list (can be automated)

---

### WHAT PARTICIPANTS NEED IN ADVANCE

Sent to all Tier 3 participants 7 days before each session:

- The question collection form link
- A one-paragraph reminder of the month's theme and which written lessons it connects to
- The Zoom link (or equivalent)
- The preparation task specified at the end of the previous session
- A note confirming replay availability for those who cannot attend live

---

### CONNECTION TO COURSE CONTENT

Each session is explicitly anchored to the written and video curriculum by month. The host references specific lesson numbers during the recap ("In Body Lesson 3, we covered circadian entrainment. Tonight we're going to work through the questions that have come up when people try to implement that in practice."). This reinforces the value of the written content and gives participants a reason to complete the lessons before each call — they'll get more from the Q&A if they have.

---

### EXPECTED OUTCOMES

By the end of 12 monthly sessions, participants will have:
- Had their specific implementation questions answered at least once in each major curriculum area
- Identified and corrected at least one significant protocol error through live feedback
- Developed a clearer understanding of which variables in their system are the primary rate-limiters for progress
- Built enough familiarity with the host's frameworks to self-diagnose more effectively between calls

## PART TWO: QUARTERLY WORKSHOPS
### 4 Sessions Per Year | 90 Minutes Each

---

### PURPOSE AND POSITIONING

Where the Monthly Q&A is reactive — answering what participants bring — the Quarterly Workshop is proactive. Each quarter, the host leads participants through a structured deep-dive into one integration challenge that consistently limits progress at the advanced level. The workshop combines teaching with live practice and guided work, not just discussion.

Quarterly Workshops are the premium live format. They are longer, more structured, and more demanding — both for the host and for participants. They deliver something the written content cannot: a guided session with real-time support.

---

### FOUR QUARTERLY THEMES

**Q1 — Body Optimisation Sprint**
*Integration challenge: Most practitioners have imprecise physiological inputs — diet, HRV tracking, and sleep architecture data that is inconsistently collected and never synthesised.*

**Q2 — Mind Regulation Protocol**
*Integration challenge: Attention breaks down under pressure — during induction, during the WBTB window, and during the dream state itself. Most participants have not built a systematic attention training protocol.*

**Q3 — Advanced WILD Intensification**
*Integration challenge: Practitioners plateau after initial success. They have occasional lucid dreams but cannot achieve the consistency, duration, and control that defines advanced practice.*

**Q4 — Annual Review and System Reset**
*Integration challenge: Without a structured annual audit, practitioners accumulate protocol complexity without ever auditing what is and isn't working. Systems become bloated and drift from the core mechanisms.*

---

### WORKSHOP STRUCTURE (90 MINUTES)

**30 minutes — Teaching Block**

This is not a lecture. It is a structured walkthrough of the specific mechanism or system behind this quarter's integration challenge. Depth over breadth. The goal is to give participants enough mechanistic understanding that the practice session that follows makes sense at a level beyond instruction-following.

Format:
- 5 minutes: frame the integration challenge — why this is the limiting factor for most practitioners at this stage
- 20 minutes: mechanism walkthrough — the relevant neuroscience, physiology, or cognitive science behind the challenge and the solution
- 5 minutes: set up the practice session — explain exactly what participants will be doing and why each step matters

Use slides for this block. Visuals for physiological mechanisms (HRV curves, sleep architecture diagrams, attention models) significantly improve comprehension.

**45 minutes — Live Practice or Guided Session**

This is the format differentiator. Each quarterly workshop includes a live guided session tailored to the quarter's focus. Participants are not passive — they are actively doing something during this block.

See per-quarter breakdown below for specifics.

**15 minutes — Integration**

A structured debrief, not an open discussion. The host guides participants through:
- A self-report (what they noticed during the practice — 2-3 minutes of silent reflection first)
- 3-5 participants share aloud (voluntary, host can invite specific people if the room is quiet)
- Host synthesis: what the reports reveal about the underlying mechanism
- One concrete protocol adjustment each participant should make based on what they just experienced
- Written integration prompt (participants write their answer in the chat or their own notes): "Based on today's session, the one thing I'm changing in my protocol this week is..."

---

### Q1 WORKSHOP: BODY OPTIMISATION SPRINT

**Teaching block focus:**

The relationship between HRV, cortisol curve, sleep architecture, and lucid dream frequency. Why most practitioners' physiological data is fragmented — tracked in silos that never get synthesised into a single picture. How to read HRV trends (not single data points) as a predictor of WILD success nights.

Cover:
- HRV morning readiness as a proxy for autonomic balance and REM pressure
- The cortisol timing problem: how late-night cortisol spikes fragment final-hours REM
- Nutritional audit framework: the four dietary inputs most directly affecting REM architecture (choline substrate, cortisol buffering, melatonin precursors, blood glucose stability overnight)
- How to build a single-page physiological dashboard that integrates all four inputs

**Live practice block (45 minutes):**

Guided physiological audit. Participants come prepared (see advance materials below). The session works through the audit framework in real time:

- 10 minutes: HRV data review. Participants share their last 30-day HRV average and trend (not individual numbers — just direction and magnitude of variation). Host identifies common patterns.
- 15 minutes: Nutritional audit walkthrough. Using a structured checklist (distributed in advance), participants rate their current dietary inputs against the four key categories. Host walks through each category with the group.
- 10 minutes: Protocol gap identification. Each participant identifies their clearest gap — the single nutritional or physiological input most likely to be limiting their practice.
- 10 minutes: Protocol prescription exercise. Participants draft a specific, measurable change they will implement for the next 30 days. Host reviews 2-3 participant examples live.

**Integration:** Participants write one 30-day protocol change. Host collects via chat for follow-up in Month 3 Q&A.

**Advance materials required:**
- 30 days of HRV data (any tracking app: Oura, Whoop, Garmin, manual HRV4Training)
- A rough 3-day diet log (doesn't have to be precise — patterns matter more than macros)
- The physiological audit checklist (distributed by host 48 hours before the session)

---

### Q2 WORKSHOP: MIND REGULATION PROTOCOL

**Teaching block focus:**

Attention architecture in the WBTB window and during the hypnagogic transition. The neuroscience of meta-awareness — why most practitioners' attention collapses at the precise moment lucidity is most accessible. The role of the anterior prefrontal cortex and why it requires both activation and stability, not just effort.

Cover:
- The attention-arousal trade-off: too alert = waking up; too relaxed = losing awareness into sleep. Where to target on this spectrum and how
- How emotional regulation precedes attentional control — why an unregulated nervous system cannot maintain the narrow arousal window required for WILD
- The three failure modes most common in advanced practitioners: over-efforting, passive drifting, and expectation-driven anxiety
- A systematic attention training protocol that can be practiced daily in 10-15 minutes (not just during WBTB)

**Live practice block (45 minutes):**

Guided attention session. Participants stay in their chairs (no need to be in bed — this is cognitive training, not induction).

- 5 minutes: baseline attention check — participants rate their current attentional stability on a 1-10 scale and note where it tends to break down (at the hypnagogic threshold, during the WBTB re-entry, or during the dream itself)
- 15 minutes: guided open monitoring practice. Host leads a structured awareness session — not meditation in a spiritual sense, but a systematic attention protocol. The instruction is precise and physiologically grounded. Participants practice maintaining meta-awareness while allowing mental content to arise and pass without following it.
- 10 minutes: deliberate distraction + recovery practice. Host introduces planned disruptions (verbal cues, a question mid-session) and guides participants through the process of noticing attentional drift and returning — the exact skill needed at the hypnagogic threshold.
- 15 minutes: verbal debriefs from 3-4 participants. What did they notice? Where did attention drift? What brought it back? Host uses these reports to demonstrate the common patterns.

**Integration:** Participants identify their primary failure mode and design a 2-week daily practice to address it specifically.

**Advance materials required:**
- No special equipment — participants need a quiet space and 90 minutes of uninterrupted time
- Review of Mind lessons 1-4 from the written curriculum (attention training and emotional regulation modules)
- A brief written note on where their attention most commonly breaks down during induction (distributed as a short prompt 48 hours before)

---

### Q3 WORKSHOP: ADVANCED WILD INTENSIFICATION

**Teaching block focus:**

Breaking through the plateau. Why practitioners who have mastered basic induction hit a ceiling — and what the ceiling actually is. The neuroscience of dream duration, stability, and control as distinct skills, not as a single continuum of progress.

Cover:
- The three dimensions of advanced lucid dream practice: frequency, duration, and intentional control (these require different training inputs)
- DEILD (Dream-Exit Initiated Lucid Dreaming) as the primary tool for chaining lucid dreams and extending total lucid time per night
- The stabilisation problem: why newly lucid dreams destabilise and how to intervene at the neural and phenomenological level
- Advanced reality testing — not the basic "count your fingers" approach, but a systematic protocol for maintaining dual awareness throughout the dream
- Using the hypnagogic state as a training environment (independent of WBTB — short afternoon practice sessions)

**Live practice block (45 minutes):**

Hypnagogic training session. This requires participants to be in a position where they can genuinely enter hypnagogia — reclining comfortably, with the ability to stay relatively still.

- 5 minutes: setup and body position check
- 30 minutes: guided hypnagogic entry and observation session. Host talks participants through the progressive stages of the hypnagogic transition, naming the visual, auditory, and somatic phenomena at each stage. Participants' task is to maintain awareness as long as possible while allowing the transition to proceed. Host provides verbal anchors every 3-4 minutes to prevent full sleep onset.
- 10 minutes: immediate debrief — participants note in writing what they experienced, at which point awareness broke down, and what phenomenology was most vivid

**Integration:** Participants identify which of the three dimensions (frequency, duration, control) is their weakest and select one specific technique from the teaching block to implement for the following 30 days.

**Advance materials required:**
- Participants need a reclining position during the session (chair, sofa, or lying on a bed)
- A quiet, darkened room is ideal — headphones recommended
- Review of Soul lessons 1-3 from the written curriculum (advanced induction and DEILD)
- Their last 30-day dream journal summary (number of lucid dreams, average duration estimate, any notes on loss of lucidity)

---

### Q4 WORKSHOP: ANNUAL REVIEW AND SYSTEM RESET

**Teaching block focus:**

Protocol auditing methodology. How to distinguish between a practice that needs more time and a practice that has a systematic error. The difference between complexity and effectiveness. Why most practitioners' systems have accumulated inputs (supplements, techniques, tracking metrics) that are untested, uncorrelated, and potentially counterproductive.

Cover:
- The signal-to-noise problem in self-tracking: how to identify which variables are actually predictive of your practice outcomes
- The minimum effective protocol concept: what is the simplest version of your current system that would produce the same outcomes?
- How to run a personal n=1 protocol experiment with proper controls (one variable at a time, defined measurement period, outcome metrics specified in advance)
- Annual metric review: what to measure and how to interpret a year's worth of dream journal and tracking data

**Live practice block (45 minutes):**

Annual audit session. Participants come prepared with their year's data.

- 10 minutes: data sharing. Host asks 3-4 participants to share their headline numbers (lucid dreams per month average, HRV trend over the year, supplements currently in use). No analysis yet — just data display.
- 15 minutes: the complexity audit. Participants complete a structured worksheet (distributed in advance) listing every current protocol element — supplements, techniques, timing interventions, tracking tools — and rate each against two criteria: (1) evidence of personal effectiveness (do their numbers improve when using this?), and (2) cost/burden of maintaining it.
- 10 minutes: the cut list. Participants identify at least two protocol elements to remove or pause for the next 90 days. Host discusses why removal is often the most impactful intervention.
- 10 minutes: the 90-day focus. Participants select one single metric to prioritise improving in Q1 of the new cycle and specify exactly how they will measure progress.

**Integration:** Participants write their 90-day protocol document — a single page with their simplified, focused protocol for the coming quarter.

**Advance materials required:**
- Year's dream journal (or summary — number of lucid dreams per month if tracking is incomplete)
- Full current protocol list (every supplement, technique, and intervention currently in use)
- The audit worksheet (distributed by host 48 hours before)
- Annual tracking data from any wearable or app (optional but strongly encouraged)

---

**Two weeks before:**
- [ ] Review the quarter's teaching content in depth — the workshop teaching block should go beyond the written curriculum, not just repeat it
- [ ] Prepare slides for the teaching block (aim for 8-12 slides, not more)

## PART THREE: ANNUAL 3-DAY VIRTUAL RETREAT
### 1 Per Year | 30 Seats Maximum | 4 Hours Per Day

---

### PURPOSE AND POSITIONING

The Annual Retreat is the apex of the Tier 3 live programme. It is not a longer workshop. It is a qualitatively different experience — sustained immersion across three consecutive days, with each day dedicated to one pillar of the Body-Mind-Soul framework.

The 30-seat maximum is a hard limit, not a marketing device. Above 30 participants, the retreat format loses its capacity for meaningful live interaction, guided group sessions, and host attention. Scarcity is structural, not artificial.

The retreat justifies Tier 3's price point at the level of experience, not information. Participants can get information from the written content and the monthly calls. The retreat gives them something the curriculum cannot: three consecutive days of focused practice, accountability, and real-time course correction with a group of advanced practitioners working at the same level.

---

### APPLICATION AND SEAT ALLOCATION PROCESS

**Application window:** Opens 60 days before the retreat. Closes when 30 applications are received or at the 30-day-before deadline, whichever comes first.

**Application form fields:**
1. How long have you been practicing WILD (months/years)?
2. What is your current average lucid dream frequency (per month)?
3. What is your primary current limitation in practice?
4. What specific outcome would you most like to achieve from the retreat?
5. Confirm you have completed Tier 1 and Tier 2 content (yes/no)
6. Confirm you can attend all three days at the scheduled times (yes/no)

**Selection criteria (in priority order):**
1. Completion of Tier 1 and Tier 2 content (non-negotiable prerequisite)
2. Confirmed availability for all three days (commitment signals investment)
3. Specificity and quality of the application (practitioners who can articulate their limitation and desired outcome will get more from the retreat)
4. Distribution across experience levels — the cohort should include a range from "consistent beginner" to "advanced practitioner" to generate useful peer learning

**Waitlist:** Maintain a waitlist of up to 15 applicants. Seats opened by confirmed dropouts (up to 14 days before) go to the waitlist in application order.

**Confirmation communication:**
- Acceptance email: sent within 7 days of application, confirms seat, provides pre-retreat materials list and schedule
- Preparation email sequence: 4 emails over the 30 days before the retreat (Day -30, Day -14, Day -7, Day -1) covering materials to prepare, practice to complete, technology setup, and the day-by-day schedule

---

### PRE-RETREAT PREPARATION REQUIREMENTS

Sent to all confirmed participants with the acceptance email:

**Required before Day 1:**
- Minimum 30 days of HRV data (any continuous tracking tool)
- A 30-day dream journal summary: total lucid dreams, average duration estimate, dominant induction method, most common failure points
- Current full protocol list (every supplement, technique, timing intervention)
- A brief written answer (200 words max) to: "What is the single most specific thing you want to be different about your practice after this retreat?"

**Technology setup:**
- Zoom (latest version, tested and working)
- Quiet, temperature-controlled room for all four hours each day
- A reclining position available in the room for the guided sessions (Day 2 and Day 3 will require this)
- Notebook and pen (physical — not digital — for tracking in-session observations)
- Wearable HRV monitor or manual HRV tracking method ready for Day 1

**Optional but strongly recommended:**
- No alcohol for 5 days before and during the retreat (significant REM suppressor)
- Consistent sleep schedule for the 7 days prior
- Review all relevant written course content for your weakest pillar (the pillar you rated lowest in your application)

---

### DAY 1: BODY RESET (4 HOURS)

**Theme:** Physiological audit, HRV baseline establishment, circadian recalibration

**Objective:** Every participant leaves Day 1 with a clear picture of their current physiological inputs and a specific, evidence-based adjustment to their nutrition, HRV monitoring, and circadian timing for the following 30 days.

---

**Hour 1 (0:00–1:00) — HRV Audit and Physiological Baseline**

*0:00–0:10 — Retreat orientation*
Host opens with the retreat structure, norms (cameras on throughout, chat for non-urgent questions, commitment to all three days), and a brief framing of why the Body pillar comes first. The physiological substrate is the foundation on which everything else is built. If the body is not optimised, technique refinement has a ceiling.

*0:10–0:40 — Group HRV data review*
All 30 participants have submitted their 30-day HRV data in advance. Host displays anonymised aggregate data (range, distribution, trends) for the group. Each participant then shares their own pattern in the chat (3-4 data points: 30-day average, recent trend direction, best night's reading, worst night's reading).

Host identifies: the 5 highest-HRV participants (what are they doing?) and the 5 lowest-HRV participants (what patterns appear?). This is not a performance ranking — it is a data-driven way to identify what environmental and lifestyle factors are most strongly associated with autonomic balance in this specific population.

*0:40–1:00 — Circadian timing audit*
Each participant completes a short circadian audit worksheet (distributed in advance): sleep and wake times over the past 2 weeks, light exposure timing, exercise timing, largest meal timing. Host reviews the aggregate patterns. Common errors identified and explained mechanistically (e.g., late light exposure suppressing melatonin onset, delaying circadian phase and reducing early-morning REM pressure).

---

**Hour 2 (1:00–2:00) — Nutritional Audit Deep-Dive**

*1:00–1:30 — Guided nutritional review*
Using the 3-day diet logs submitted in advance, host leads the group through the four primary nutritional inputs affecting WILD practice: choline substrate, cortisol buffering agents, melatonin precursors (tryptophan pathway), and overnight blood glucose stability.

Each participant rates their current intake against optimal ranges for each category. Host provides specific benchmarks and explains the mechanism behind each one (connecting to the Body written curriculum for participants who want more depth).

*1:30–2:00 — Supplementation audit*
Participants share their current supplement stacks (submitted in advance). Host reviews the most common stacks in the group, identifies over-supplementation patterns (more inputs does not mean better outcomes), and walks through the evidence base for each commonly used compound:
- Galantamine: dosing, timing, cycling protocol — the evidence and the failure modes
- Alpha-GPC vs CDP-choline: when each is appropriate, how to assess your response
- Vitamin B6 / P5P: the REM-enhancement mechanism and realistic effect size
- Phosphatidylserine: cortisol modulation and the evidence for evening dosing
- 5-HTP / tryptophan: the serotonin-REM relationship and why timing matters more than dose

---

**Hour 3 (2:00–3:00) — Individual Protocol Design**

*2:00–2:40 — Personal protocol design session*
Each participant drafts their revised physiological protocol for the next 30 days, addressing: one dietary adjustment, one circadian timing change, one supplement change (addition, removal, or dosing adjustment). The rule: no more than one change per category. Protocol complexity is the enemy of accurate self-assessment.

Host circulates through breakout rooms (3-5 participants per room, 20 minutes) where participants share their draft protocols for peer feedback. One host rotation through each room to give direct feedback.

*2:40–3:00 — Reconvene and Q&A*
Return to main room. Host takes 5-6 live questions on anything from the first three hours. Clarifications, edge cases, individual exceptions.

---

**Hour 4 (3:00–4:00) — Guided Practice and Day 1 Close**

*3:00–3:40 — Guided body scan and HRV session*
A structured physiological awareness session. Not relaxation for its own sake — a deliberate practice of interoceptive awareness. Participants in a reclining position, guided by the host through a systematic body scan protocol that mirrors the pre-sleep physiological preparation from the Body curriculum. The goal is to establish a clear felt sense of their current physiological state as a reference point for the next two days.

*3:40–3:55 — Day 1 debrief*
Participants write in their notebooks: (1) the three most useful things from today, (2) their specific protocol change for the next 30 days, (3) one question they still have for Day 2 or Day 3.

*3:55–4:00 — Day 2 preview*
Brief explanation of Day 2 structure, what participants need to prepare overnight, and any materials to have ready.

**Day 1 preparation task for overnight:**
- Note their dream upon waking (whatever is available — even fragments)
- Check and log their HRV upon waking before getting out of bed
- Identify one emotional or cognitive pattern they notice as a recurring feature of their waking experience (relevant to Day 2's emotional regulation work)

---

### DAY 2: MIND WORK (4 HOURS)

**Theme:** Emotional regulation audit, breathwork intensive, attention architecture

**Objective:** Every participant leaves Day 2 with a clear picture of how their emotional state and attentional patterns interact with their lucid dreaming practice, and a specific attention training protocol to implement.

---

**Hour 1 (0:00–1:00) — Overnight Report and Emotional Audit**

*0:00–0:15 — Overnight report*
Participants share: HRV reading on waking, any dream recall, and any observation from the Day 1 overnight preparation task. Host identifies patterns across the group — are HRV readings trending after one day of protocol adjustment? What is the predominant emotional tone in the overnight reports?

*0:15–1:00 — Emotional regulation audit*
Lucid dream stability correlates directly with emotional regulation capacity. The dream state amplifies emotional arousal — which is why practitioners who have not done systematic emotional regulation work frequently lose lucidity during intense or threatening dream sequences.

The audit framework covers:
- Baseline emotional reactivity: how quickly does the participant return to baseline after an emotional trigger? (measured in minutes/hours, not on a vague scale)
- Rumination index: how often do intrusive thoughts interfere with pre-sleep focus?
- Anxiety-induction patterns: does performance anxiety around induction attempts reduce success rates?
- Dream-specific emotional triggers: what is the most common emotional experience that breaks lucidity?

Participants complete this audit individually in writing (15 minutes), then host facilitates a group share (not compulsory — participants share what they are willing to share). Host maps responses to specific regulatory interventions, not general advice.

---

**Hour 2 (1:00–2:00) — Breathwork Intensive**

*1:00–2:00 — Guided breathwork session*
This is the most physically demanding element of the retreat. Participants need a mat, floor space, or reclining surface, and a clear 60 minutes with no interruptions.

The session covers three breathwork protocols in sequence, each with a specific physiological purpose relevant to the WILD practice:

**Protocol 1 — Coherent Breathing (20 minutes)**
5.5 breaths per minute (approximately 5.5 seconds inhale, 5.5 seconds exhale). This pattern is associated with maximum HRV amplitude — the respiratory frequency at which the cardiovascular and respiratory systems resonate. The direct application: practicing this before WBTB lowers baseline cortisol, raises parasympathetic tone, and optimises the autonomic state for REM re-entry. Host guides with verbal timing cues and explains the mechanism as participants practice.

**Protocol 2 — Box Breathing Variant for Arousal Regulation (20 minutes)**
4-4-4-4 (inhale 4, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4). Not the military stress-management version — here applied specifically to the WBTB re-entry window where participants are attempting to lower arousal without losing awareness. The double hold trains the participant to tolerate stillness without either increasing effort (which raises arousal) or disengaging (which causes sleep onset without awareness).

**Protocol 3 — Extended Exhale for Sleep-Onset Optimisation (20 minutes)**
4-count inhale, 8-count exhale (ratio 1:2). Activates the parasympathetic branch more strongly than balanced breathing. Used in the final phase of WBTB re-entry when the practitioner is within minutes of sleep onset and needs to lower arousal without active effort. Participants practice the specific cognitive state that accompanies this breathing pattern — receptive, alert, non-grasping.

Host provides verbal guidance throughout all three protocols. Participants note which protocol produced the most pronounced physiological response.

---

**Hour 3 (2:00–3:00) — Attention Architecture**

*2:00–2:30 — Attention training framework*
Delivery of the core cognitive model: the dual-process nature of attention in advanced WILD practice. Participants at this stage need two concurrent attention modes operating simultaneously during induction:
- Focused attention: the anchor (breath, body sensation, intention phrase) that maintains continuity through the hypnagogic transition
- Open monitoring: the background awareness that observes the entire field — including the hypnagogic imagery — without collapsing into it

Most practitioners are doing one or the other. The goal is both simultaneously. Host walks through the neural basis, common failure modes, and the training method.

*2:30–3:00 — Live attention practice*
A 30-minute guided dual-process attention session. Participants in their reclining position, guided by the host through the dual-attention protocol. Verbal anchors every 4-5 minutes. Participants note in real time (immediately after the session, not during) what they observed, where the balance between focused and open attention was maintained, and where it broke down.

---

**Hour 4 (3:00–4:00) — Integration and Attention Protocol Design**

*3:00–3:20 — Breakout rooms*
Participants share their attention session observations in groups of 3-4. What did each person notice? What was the most difficult moment? Where did the transition from focused to open monitoring feel natural vs. forced?

*3:20–3:45 — Personal attention training protocol design*
Each participant designs a 10-15 minute daily attention practice they will use for the next 30 days. Protocol must specify: timing (when in the day), duration, the specific technique, and how they will know it's working (one measurable outcome — e.g., induction attempts where awareness is maintained to the hypnagogic stage).

*3:45–3:55 — Day 2 debrief*
Three written reflections: (1) the most useful element of today, (2) the specific daily practice they are committing to, (3) one question for Day 3.

*3:55–4:00 — Day 3 preview*
Day 3 is the most demanding. Participants should sleep on their regular schedule tonight, note everything on waking, and come prepared for the extended practice session.

**Day 2 overnight preparation:**
- Practice the coherent breathing protocol for 10 minutes before sleep
- Set a WBTB alarm 5.5 hours after sleep onset and attempt the WILD protocol using the dual-attention technique practiced today
- Note outcome (success, partial, failure) and all observations immediately on waking

### DAY 3: ADVANCED PRACTICE INTENSIVE (4 HOURS)

**Theme:** Extended WILD practice session, group debrief, advanced technique workshop

**Objective:** Every participant leaves Day 3 with a direct experience of advanced practice under guidance, clear data on their current ceiling, and a specific advanced technique to develop in the following 30 days.

---

**Hour 1 (0:00–1:00) — Overnight WBTB Report and Advanced Technique Workshop**

*0:00–0:20 — Overnight WBTB report*
All participants share their WBTB attempt from last night: success/partial/no lucidity, what technique they used, at what point awareness broke down (if it did), HRV reading on waking. Host identifies patterns. How many participants achieved lucidity using the dual-attention technique from Day 2? What were the common failure points?

This is live data from 30 practitioners who all attempted the same protocol on the same night. This kind of aggregated real-time self-report is not available in any written curriculum — it is one of the unique features of the retreat format.

*0:20–1:00 — Advanced technique workshop: DEILD and chaining*
For practitioners who have mastered basic WILD induction, the primary frontier is consecutive lucid dreams within a single night and extended lucid dream duration. DEILD (Dream-Exit Initiated Lucid Dreaming) is the most effective tool for both.

Host covers:
- The DEILD mechanics: waking from a dream with minimal movement, maintaining the dream memory, and re-entering directly into a new lucid dream within 60-90 seconds
- Why DEILD has a higher success rate than WILD for practitioners who already have established dream recall
- The movement threshold: how still is still enough, and what happens neurologically if the practitioner moves before re-entry
- Stabilisation during DEILD re-entry: the first 10-15 seconds of a chained lucid dream are the most fragile
- Reality testing protocols that work for DEILD vs. those that are counterproductive (any technique that requires high cognitive effort can break the re-entry)
- Building a DEILD chain: what to do during the brief waking interval to maximise the probability of the next entry

---

**Hour 2 (1:00–2:00) — Extended Guided Practice Session**

The primary event of Day 3. This is a 60-minute extended guided practice session. The design accounts for the fact that the body and mind have been primed over the previous two days — physiological and attentional baselines are now better calibrated than at the start of the retreat.

Participants need: reclining position, darkened room if possible, no external interruptions.

*1:00–1:10 — Setup and intention setting*
Participants write in their notebook: the specific intention for this session (not just "have a lucid dream" — what do they want to do once lucid?). They also note their current physiological state (HRV reading from the morning, energy level, any notable physical sensations).

*1:10–2:00 — Guided extended WILD session*
Host guides the group through a full WILD attempt with extended hypnagogic guidance. The structure:

- Minutes 0-10: physiological settling. Coherent breathing protocol. Host narrates the transition from waking physiological baseline to sleep-entry state.
- Minutes 10-25: hypnagogic entry. Host provides verbal anchors every 2-3 minutes — naming the common hypnagogic experiences (phosphene activity, auditory hallucinations, body sensations, myoclonic jerks) as they arise. This normalises the experiences that typically startle practitioners out of the transition.
- Minutes 25-45: transition window. Host drops verbal frequency to every 5-6 minutes. Participants are now in the deepest phase of the session. The host's role is to prevent complete sleep onset without awareness — not to maintain high engagement.
- Minutes 45-50: gentle return. Host brings participants back to full waking awareness gradually.
- Minutes 50-60: immediate written debrief (while the experience is fresh). Participants note: what stage they reached, what phenomenology they observed, where (if) awareness broke down, and what the content was if they achieved any dream-like experience.

---

**Hour 3 (2:00–3:00) — Group Debrief and Advanced Diagnostics**

*2:00–2:30 — Full group debrief*
Every participant shares a brief report from the practice session: what happened, what stage they reached, what was most notable. Host categorises reports in real time:
- Group A: achieved full WILD or near-WILD (what did they do that others didn't?)
- Group B: reached hypnagogic but lost awareness (what specifically happened at the transition?)
- Group C: remained fully alert and did not enter hypnagogia (what is the diagnosis? Most likely: excessive effort, anxiety, or caffeine/stimulant interference)

Host uses the debrief to demonstrate how to diagnose practice outcomes from first-person report — the skill of accurate self-assessment that is the most important meta-skill in advanced practice.

*2:30–3:00 — Advanced diagnostics workshop*
Based on the debrief data, host delivers 30 minutes of targeted diagnostics for the most common patterns that emerged. This cannot be scripted in advance — it is responsive to what actually happened in the practice session. However, common patterns to prepare for include:

- "I get close but always wake up at the transition point": the arousal regulation problem — explains the mechanism and the specific intervention
- "I fell asleep immediately and lost awareness": the opposite problem — too much relaxation, insufficient anchor. Intervention: stronger intention, louder internal anchor phrase, slightly higher arousal state before entry
- "I had vivid hypnagogic imagery but couldn't enter it": the boundary problem — explains why the imagery is present but entry isn't occurring and the specific technique for dissolving the boundary
- "My body sensations were too intense and I disengaged": the vibration/sleep paralysis fear response — normalisation and the specific protocol for maintaining awareness through intense proprioceptive phenomena

---

**Hour 4 (3:00–4:00) — 30-Day Protocol, Community, and Close**

*3:00–3:30 — 30-day post-retreat protocol*
Each participant designs their focused 30-day protocol based on the three days of practice and feedback. The protocol document is one page maximum and covers:
- Physiological inputs: the specific dietary, HRV, and circadian adjustments from Day 1
- Daily attention practice: the 10-15 minute protocol from Day 2
- Primary technique: the induction method to use for all attempts in the next 30 days (no switching)
- One advanced technique to develop: based on Day 3 diagnostics
- Measurement: exactly how they will track progress (dream journal minimum; HRV correlation optional but encouraged)

Participants share their protocol document in the retreat chat or designated shared space.

*3:30–3:45 — Retreat debrief and community handoff*
Open floor for final reflections. Host asks each participant to share one sentence: "The most valuable thing from these three days was..."

Host announces the post-retreat integration period:
- A dedicated post-retreat thread in the Discord/Telegram community where participants can share updates, ask follow-up questions, and report outcomes from their 30-day protocol
- A 30-day check-in in the next Monthly Q&A session where retreat participants get priority questions

*3:45–4:00 — Close*
Host closes with a clear restatement of what participants now have: a precise physiological protocol, a daily attention practice, a primary technique, an advanced development target, and a community of 30 practitioners who went through the same three days.

The close is direct, not ceremonial. The measure of this retreat is what participants do in the 30 days after it. That is the only metric that matters.

### WHAT YOU HAVE ACCESS TO

Tier 3 includes three live formats that run throughout the year. Here is what each one is, what it is not, and what you need to do to get value from it.

**Monthly Q&A Sessions — 12 per year**

Once per month, there is a live 60-minute call. The first 10 minutes cover the month's theme in a tight, structured recap. The next 45 minutes are dedicated to questions — both submitted in advance and asked live. The final 5 minutes set up next month.

You will receive a question collection form 7 days before each session. Submit your question before the 48-hour deadline and it will be considered for the call. If your question is not addressed live, the replay will often cover the underlying issue when similar questions from other participants are answered.

Every session is recorded and available on the course platform the same day.

The monthly themes map to the curriculum: the first three months focus on Body, Mind, and Soul track fundamentals respectively, then the cycle deepens through the year. You do not need to have joined at the beginning to benefit — all replays are available and the live rotation is ongoing.

**Quarterly Workshops — 4 per year**

Four times per year, there is a 90-minute structured workshop. These are not extended Q&A sessions — they have a specific format: 30 minutes of teaching, 45 minutes of live practice or guided work, and 15 minutes of integration.

The four workshop themes are fixed: Body Optimisation Sprint (Q1), Mind Regulation Protocol (Q2), Advanced WILD Intensification (Q3), and Annual Review and System Reset (Q4).

Come prepared. Each workshop has advance materials that need to be completed before the session. You will receive these 48 hours before each workshop. If you arrive without the required data (HRV history, diet log, protocol list, depending on the quarter), you will not be able to participate fully in the practice session.

**Annual 3-Day Virtual Retreat — 1 per year, 30 seats**

The retreat is the apex of the live programme. It runs across three consecutive days, four hours each day, via Zoom. Day 1 is Body, Day 2 is Mind, Day 3 is advanced practice intensive.

Seats are application-based and limited to 30. There are more Tier 3 members than seats, so apply early when the window opens. Selection is based on your commitment to the full three days and the quality of your application — specifically, how clearly you can articulate your current practice limitation.

The retreat requires significant preparation. Plan for it two weeks in advance, not two days.

### YOUR FIRST 30 DAYS

**Week 1:**

- Access the course platform and locate the live programme section. All past replay recordings are available immediately.
- Check the community channel for the current month's upcoming Q&A date. If one is within your first 30 days, submit a question — even if it feels basic. The Q&A is the fastest way to have your specific situation addressed.

**Week 3:**

- Identify the one written curriculum area you are weakest in. This is the pillar to prioritise before your first live session.
- If you have not already, post in the community channel and introduce yourself. The 30 practitioners in each retreat cohort are selected partly for the quality of peer interaction they generate. The community is where that happens between retreat years.

**Week 4:**

- By the end of your first month, you should have: one Q&A session attended or replayed, your current practice protocol documented on paper, and a clear understanding of which monthly theme cycle you are in.
- Your preparation task for Month 2's Q&A will have arrived. Complete it before the 48-hour deadline.

### WHAT THE LIVE PROGRAMME IS NOT

The live sessions are not where you learn the fundamentals. The written curriculum and video content are where you learn the framework. The live sessions are where you apply it, troubleshoot it, and refine it with real-time feedback.

Do not come to the Q&A without having engaged with the relevant written content. Do not come to the workshops without the required advance materials. Do not apply for the retreat if you cannot commit to all three days.

The live programme rewards preparation. Practitioners who arrive with data, specific questions, and a clear picture of their current limitations get disproportionately more from each session than those who arrive without it.

---

### ONE EXPECTATION TO SET NOW

Progress in this practice is not linear. There will be months where your lucid dream frequency increases sharply, and months where it plateaus. The live programme is designed to address both — but it cannot do that if you only engage when things are going well.

The most useful Q&A questions are not "how do I get started?" — those are answered in the written content. The most useful questions are "here is my data, here is what I am doing, and here is exactly where it breaks down." That level of specificity is what the live sessions are built for.

Show up with your data. That is all.
