## B1: The Sleep-Nutrition Axis

*[Calm, direct delivery. Start mid-thought — no preamble. Visible desk with simple food items optional but not required. Pace is measured, not rushed.]*

Your brain cannot make melatonin without tryptophan. And it cannot make tryptophan — it can only get it from food. That's not a soft, wellness statement. That is the literal biochemistry of your sleep system. The melatonin that initiates your sleep, that times your sleep stages, that creates the conditions for extended REM — it begins as an amino acid in whatever you ate for dinner.

So the question is not "is food relevant to sleep?" That question is settled. The question is: which foods, at what times, doing what to your system?

Let's start with the pathway because if you understand the pathway, the protocols make sense.

Tryptophan is an amino acid found in turkey, eggs, dairy, salmon, pumpkin seeds, tofu. You consume it. In the presence of tryptophan hydroxylase — an enzyme that requires B6 and iron as cofactors — it gets converted to 5-HTP. From 5-HTP, you get serotonin. From serotonin, as darkness falls and the pineal gland activates, you get N-acetylserotonin and then melatonin. This is not a metaphor about "serotonin making you happy." This is a biosynthesis chain. Interrupt it at any point and your melatonin production drops.

Here's the part most people don't know: getting tryptophan from your food into your brain is a competitive process. Tryptophan has to cross the blood-brain barrier using a transporter it shares with several other large amino acids — leucine, isoleucine, valine, phenylalanine. If your meal is high in protein without carbohydrate, those other amino acids flood the transporter first. Tryptophan loses. Less gets to the brain. Less melatonin.

Carbohydrates fix this. Not a lot of carbohydrates — a moderate amount. When carbohydrates raise insulin, insulin drives branched-chain amino acids into muscle tissue. The transporter clears. Tryptophan wins. A mixed meal — moderate carbohydrate, a tryptophan-containing protein source — delivers more tryptophan to the brain than a high-protein, low-carb meal even if the protein content is identical.

Timing the meal matters too. The conversion chain from tryptophan to melatonin takes several hours. If you want melatonin synthesis peaking over your sleep onset, you need to eat roughly three to four hours before bed. Eat the tryptophan-containing meal at 9pm for an 11pm sleep and you're eating too late — the synthesis peak arrives at 2am when you're already in your second sleep cycle. Eat at 6:30pm for a 10:30pm sleep and you've timed it correctly.

Now — what destroys this system from the other direction. What actively suppresses REM depth.

Alcohol. This one is worth dwelling on because it is genuinely misunderstood. Alcohol makes you feel drowsy and it does help you fall asleep. The problem is that sleep onset is not sleep architecture. Alcohol is a GABAergic sedative that suppresses REM in the first half of the night. The brain gets through NREM but doesn't have its normal REM episodes in the early cycles. Then, as blood alcohol falls across the second half of the night, you get a REM rebound — fragmented, intense REM that wakes you up, generates vivid and often anxiety-tinged dreams, and leaves you feeling unrestored. One glass of wine at dinner might have a negligible effect. One glass within four hours of sleep measurably disrupts your second-half REM. For a lucid dreaming practice that depends entirely on late-night REM extension, alcohol is a disqualifier.

High-glycaemic carbohydrates before bed. This one is about glucose dynamics, and it gets its own lesson later in the track. For now: a refined carbohydrate meal within ninety minutes of bed creates a glucose spike, then a compensatory insulin overshoot, then a glucose trough. That trough triggers cortisol. And cortisol is an arousal hormone — it is your stress response, your emergency system. It does not belong at 3am fragmenting your REM. Avoid white rice, refined pasta, pastries, juice, or high-sugar foods in the last two hours before sleep.

Large meals too late. The thermodynamics are relevant here. Digesting a large meal generates heat — this is the thermic effect of food. Deep sleep and REM both require a drop in core body temperature. If you're still thermally elevated from a 9pm dinner, your CBT hasn't dropped by the time you try to sleep at 11pm. Sleep onset is delayed, SWS is shallower, and the whole cascade suffers.

And caffeine deserves a mention even though it's not food. With a half-life of five to seven hours, a 3pm coffee still has half its concentration in your system at 8pm, and a quarter by 1am. Caffeine blocks adenosine receptors. Adenosine is the sleep-pressure molecule that drives deep SWS. Block it and you don't get the deep slow-wave sleep in the first part of the night — and it's that deep slow-wave sleep that creates the conditions for robust REM rebound later. The practical rule: no caffeine after midday for most people. Some people process caffeine faster; some slower. Your HRV data will tell you which category you're in.

Now — the systems-level view of all of this. Your sleep system is a complex network with multiple inputs and outputs. Nutrition qualifies as a primary leverage point — one of the few upstream inputs that, when you change it, shifts downstream variables broadly. It's upstream of melatonin synthesis. It's upstream of glucose variability, which is a direct metric of system disruption. It's upstream of inflammatory tone, which is upstream of cortisol. If you're trying to optimise your WILD practice by fine-tuning your re-entry technique while leaving your nutrition disordered, you're adjusting the downstream output while the upstream input is generating interference. The signal won't hold.

Here's the practical summary. Three to four hours before bed: a mixed meal containing a tryptophan-rich protein source and moderate complex carbohydrates. Within two hours of bed: no high-glycaemic foods, no large meal volume, no alcohol. Caffeine before midday only. That's it. Those three rules don't require dietary perfectionism. They require awareness of timing and composition in the final four hours of your day.

Get the substrate right. Everything built on top of it gets better automatically.

## B2: Circadian Eating — Meal Timing as a Sleep Signal

*[Slightly more technical energy — this is systems-level thinking. Authoritative but grounded. Optional: small visual of a clock or timeline could support the WBTB timing explanation, but not necessary.]*

Most people know that light sets the body clock. Fewer people know that food sets it too — but a different body clock. And the difference between those two clocks, when they're in sync versus when they're drifting, determines a large portion of your sleep quality in ways that technique alone cannot fix.

Let me explain the architecture.

You have a master clock in your hypothalamus — a small region called the suprachiasmatic nucleus, SCN. It runs on roughly a 24-hour cycle and it's entrained primarily by light. Morning light hits your retinas, signals the SCN, and the SCN broadcasts a timing signal to the rest of your body: it is day, begin the active-phase programme.

But your liver doesn't have eyes. Neither does your gut, your muscle tissue, your immune system. These organs have their own clocks — peripheral clocks — that run on molecular feedback loops involving CLOCK genes and BMAL1. And these peripheral clocks are entrained not by light but by feeding. When you eat is, for your metabolic periphery, as powerful a time signal as sunrise.

The word for these time signals is zeitgeber — German for "time-giver." Light is the zeitgeber for the master clock. Food is the zeitgeber for peripheral clocks.

In a well-entrained system, these two signals are aligned. You're eating when it's light, fasting when it's dark, and your master clock and peripheral clocks are running in synchrony. Melatonin onset is clean and well-timed. SWS architecture is sharp. REM windows fall exactly where they should.

In a misaligned system — eating at 10pm, grazing across a 14-hour window, no clear feeding pattern — peripheral clocks drift out of phase with the master clock. Your liver thinks it's midday. Your SCN thinks it's midnight. The downstream expression of this desynchrony includes: delayed sleep onset, blunted melatonin signal, reduced SWS depth, and displaced REM timing.

That last point — displaced REM timing — is the one that matters most for this programme.

REM sleep is not just pressure-driven, it's phase-driven. The bulk of your REM happens in the second half of the night not because you've been asleep long enough but because REM propensity follows the circadian rhythm of body temperature — it peaks in the early morning hours, in the rising phase of core body temperature. Late eating delays the circadian phase. When the circadian phase shifts by ninety minutes, the REM propensity window shifts by ninety minutes. If your sleep window is fixed — you have to wake up at a certain time — that REM window gets compressed from the front. You don't lose REM from the end of the night. You lose it from the beginning of the REM-rich portion. And for WILD, which targets the fourth and fifth REM episodes of the night, compression from the front means you either catch them at a worse time or miss them entirely.

This is why a practitioner can run a perfect WBTB protocol and still find that their re-entry attempts are hitting SWS instead of REM. The technique is correct. The circadian timing is not.

So how do you fix it? Time-restricted eating — TRE. The mechanism is clean: when you restrict food intake to a defined window during the active phase of the day, peripheral clocks synchronise with the master clock. The feeding signal becomes high-amplitude and consistent. Circadian noise decreases. The melatonin signal clarifies. Sleep architecture sharpens.

Here's the protocol, built backwards from your WBTB target.

Step one: set your WBTB target time. Let's say 5am. That is your anchor. Work backwards.

Step two: for WBTB at 5am, you're probably targeting sleep onset around 9:30 to 10pm — giving you five to five-and-a-half hours before the alarm, enough for four full 90-minute cycles. Your last meal should be at least three hours before sleep onset. So: last meal by 6:30 to 7pm at the latest.

Step three: if your last meal is at 7pm, and you want a nine-to-ten hour eating window, your first meal opens at 9 or 10am. That means skipping what most people call breakfast and delaying your first food intake. Ghrelin — the hunger hormone — adapts to your eating window within one to two weeks. The hunger that currently shows up at 7am will, within a fortnight, shift to 9am. The body is adaptive; it resets its hunger timing to match the established feeding window.

Step four: hold the window consistently for fourteen days. Not perfectly — if there are two or three social occasions where you eat late, that won't destroy the calibration. The peripheral clocks have inertia. But the general pattern needs to be consistent. Consistency is the signal. Variability is the noise.

The molecular mechanism is worth knowing because it tells you this isn't just a lifestyle tweak. CLOCK and BMAL1 are gene-level transcription factors that drive the circadian oscillator in peripheral cells. They're entrained through insulin signalling, mTOR activation, and NAD+/SIRT1 pathways — all of which are activated by food intake. Consistent feeding times produce a clean, high-amplitude oscillatory signal in these pathways. Irregular feeding produces low-amplitude, desynchronised oscillations. A system running on clean oscillatory signals navigates state transitions more reliably. That is literally what TRE does at the molecular level.

Now — two practical objections. First: "I train in the mornings and need post-workout nutrition early." You can shift the window to match. If you train at 6am and finish at 7am, open your eating window at 7am and close it by 5pm or 6pm. The window just needs to align with the active phase. It doesn't need to start at 10am specifically.

Second: "Social eating means late meals sometimes." Two late meals per week within a consistent five-day protocol is tolerable for most people. The peripheral clock inertia absorbs the deviation without significant phase drift. It's when the irregular eating pattern becomes the norm — eating at 6pm two nights and 10pm three nights — that the calibration is lost.

One final point. Your readiness score includes sleep regularity as one of its three variables. Sleep regularity is downstream of circadian calibration. And circadian calibration is partially downstream of eating consistency. When you track your readiness score and see the sleep regularity metric improving over weeks, eating consistency is one of the quiet upstream variables producing that change. You may never see a direct "meal timing caused this" connection in your data. But the pattern is there.

Treat the eating window as an operating parameter, not a dietary preference. You wouldn't adjust when the alarm goes off based on how you feel each morning. You set it and you hold it. Do the same with your eating window.

## B3: Blood Sugar and Dream Depth

*[Slightly more clinical energy — this is physiology with direct biohacking implications. Concrete data references work well here. Steady, confident pacing.]*

Have you ever woken up at 3am with a racing heart and a completely alert mind, even though you were exhausted? Most people chalk this up to stress or anxiety. Some people start spiralling about what's worrying them. But there's a much more precise explanation, and it's almost entirely dietary.

That 3am waking — the alert, restless, hard-to-return-to-sleep variety — is the cortisol spike generated by your body in response to a drop in overnight blood glucose. It happens on a predictable schedule, it has a direct dietary cause, and it is fully addressable.

Here's the mechanism.

While you sleep, your brain keeps consuming glucose. The hypothalamus monitors systemic energy status throughout the night. When blood glucose drops below approximately 3.5 to 4.0 millimoles per litre, the hypothalamus triggers a counter-regulatory cascade. First glucagon from the pancreas. Then epinephrine from the adrenal medulla. And if the trough is sustained — cortisol from the adrenal cortex.

Epinephrine raises heart rate. Cortisol increases metabolic rate, body temperature, and shifts the nervous system toward sympathetic dominance. These are activating hormones. Their evolutionary purpose is to mobilise glucose stores in a perceived energy emergency. In sleep terms, they are arousal signals. The result is a micro-arousal at a minimum — a partial transition out of deep sleep — or a full waking at worst.

And what causes the glucose trough? Something you ate several hours earlier.

High-glycaemic carbohydrates consumed within ninety minutes to two hours of bed produce a predictable glucose spike. Insulin rises sharply to bring glucose back down. In a system with good insulin sensitivity and moderate glycaemic load, glucose returns to fasting baseline. In a system where glycaemic load was too high, or insulin sensitivity is impaired, glucose overshoots — it drops below fasting baseline. That trough arrives at roughly 1:30 to 3am depending on when you ate, and that is when the counter-regulatory response fires.

This isn't a clinical-population-only finding. CGM data — continuous glucose monitoring — from metabolically healthy individuals shows that nocturnal glucose variability, even within the technically "normal" range, correlates with micro-arousal frequency, reduced slow-wave sleep duration, and lower subjective sleep quality. You don't have to be diabetic or pre-diabetic to have glucose variability disrupting your sleep. You just have to eat in a way that produces it.

For WILD practitioners, the timing of the 3am spike is catastrophic. Think about your sleep architecture. If you sleep at 10pm, your fourth and fifth REM episodes — the long ones, the vivid ones, the ones you're trying to access with WBTB — are falling in the 3:30 to 6am window. The counter-regulatory cortisol spike hits you right at the transition into your deepest, most accessible REM territory. That is the most expensive disruption you can have.

So the protocol. Three principles.

One: no high-glycaemic foods in the two hours before bed. This means no refined carbohydrates — white bread, white rice, pasta, cereals, fruit juice, high-sugar snacks, anything that produces a sharp glucose spike. If you want carbohydrate before bed for the tryptophan transport effect we covered in the last module, choose low-glycaemic sources: a small bowl of oats, sweet potato, legumes. These digest slowly, produce a moderate glucose rise, and don't generate the overshoot that creates a trough.

Two: if you're eating within ninety minutes of sleep — if your schedule requires a late-ish meal — include a small amount of protein and fat. A tablespoon of almond butter, a handful of walnuts, a small portion of full-fat Greek yoghurt. Protein and fat slow gastric emptying and blunt the glycaemic response to any carbohydrate in the meal. They act as a natural glycaemic buffer. This combination produces a slow, stable glucose release across the first two to three hours of sleep rather than a spike-and-crash pattern.

Three: consider a glycine source before bed. Glycine — found in collagen protein and available as a standalone supplement at 3 grams — has two relevant effects. It's an inhibitory amino acid that lowers core body temperature by increasing peripheral blood flow. And it's a gluconeogenic substrate: it gives the liver raw material for producing glucose during the night, reducing the depth of the overnight trough. Clinical trials on glycine supplementation show measurable improvements in SWS duration and subjective sleep quality. It's not glamorous, but the evidence is solid and the cost is negligible.

Now — if you want to actually see your overnight glucose pattern, a CGM is the most direct tool available. Libre from Abbott is available without a prescription in most countries. You wear a small sensor on the back of your upper arm for fourteen days and you can read your overnight glucose trace on your phone. Cross-reference that trace with your sleep data from Oura or WHOOP or Garmin, and you have a personalised glucose-sleep correlation dataset.

What you're looking for: no readings below 4.0 mmol/L during the sleep period. Total nocturnal glucose variation less than 2 mmol/L. A trace that looks like a gentle plateau is the target. A trace with a steep drop in the 1-3am window followed by a rebound is the trough pattern — and it will show up in your sleep data as fragmentation or a mid-night waking right on schedule.

Here's the physiological framing. The readiness score has three variables. One of them is glucose variability. It's there because glucose variability is a real-time readout of how much capacity your metabolic system is spending on counter-regulation. When blood glucose is stable overnight, the hypothalamus and adrenal glands are not being recruited for emergency glucose management. That means the regulatory resources of the autonomic system are free — available for the precise stage cycling that deep, architecturally clean sleep requires. High glucose variability means those resources are consumed. Every cortisol spike generated by a glucose trough is a micro-arousal, and every micro-arousal is a potential WILD entry point destroyed.

You are not trying to "sleep better." You are trying to maintain specific neurochemical conditions across a five-to-six hour period in the second half of the night. Blood glucose stability is one of the direct preconditions for those conditions being met.

The pre-bed protocol is not complicated. Low-glycaemic carbohydrates if you need carbohydrate. A protein-fat buffer for the glycaemic response. Three grams of glycine if you want the extra insurance. And nothing high-glycaemic in the ninety minutes before sleep. Run that for two weeks and compare your 3am waking frequency to where it was before. For most people, it stops.

The glucose trough is not mysterious. It is a dietary artefact. And dietary artefacts can be corrected.

## B4: The Gut-Brain Axis

*[Engaged, slightly leaning in — this is the surprise module, the one where the frame shifts for most listeners. The gut-REM connection is counterintuitive and should be delivered with that sense of "this matters more than you realise."]*

Ninety percent. That's the number. Ninety percent of your body's serotonin is produced in the gut. Not in the brain. In the lining of your intestines, in specialised cells called enterochromaffin cells, using tryptophan — the same amino acid we discussed in B1 — as the substrate.

The serotonin that eventually becomes melatonin, the serotonin that modulates mood and anxiety, the serotonin whose signalling is disrupted in depression — most of it originates in your digestive system. And the rate at which your gut produces that serotonin is modulated by the community of bacteria, fungi, and archaea living in your intestines. Your microbiome.

This is not a metaphor. This is biochemistry. And its implications for sleep — for REM depth, for dream quality, for the neurochemical conditions required for conscious sleep state transitions — are direct.

Let me walk through the full pathway.

Specific bacterial genera in the gut — particularly Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium, and certain Clostridia species — produce short-chain fatty acids: butyrate, propionate, acetate. These SCFAs stimulate the enterochromaffin cells to synthesise and release serotonin. When microbiome diversity is high and these species are abundant, SCFA production is robust and serotonin synthesis is well-supported. When microbiome diversity is low — depleted by antibiotics, ultra-processed food, chronic stress, low fibre intake — SCFA production falls, serotonin synthesis falls, and the melatonin pathway is impaired before it even begins.

The evidence here is not speculative. Germ-free mice — animals raised in sterile conditions with no gut bacteria — have dramatically reduced serotonin levels in both gut and blood, and their sleep architecture is measurably disrupted. Human observational studies consistently show correlations between microbiome diversity scores and sleep quality, with the strongest associations in slow-wave sleep duration and REM length.

Beyond serotonin: the microbiome produces GABA via specific Lactobacillus species, modulates BDNF — brain-derived neurotrophic factor, the neuroplasticity signal that rises during sleep and drives memory consolidation in REM — and directly regulates the HPA axis, the cortisol-production pathway. A diverse, healthy microbiome is not just a digestive asset. It is a sleep architecture system operating in your abdomen.

Now — the leaky gut cascade. Because this is where things get clinically significant.

Intestinal permeability — leaky gut — happens when the tight junctions between the cells lining your gut wall become compromised. Bacteria in your intestines produce lipopolysaccharides, or LPS — fragments of their outer membranes. In a healthy, intact gut, these stay inside the gut. When tight junctions are compromised, LPS passes into systemic circulation. The immune system detects this as a threat and responds with pro-inflammatory cytokines: TNF-alpha, interleukin 1-beta, interleukin 6.

These cytokines signal the hypothalamus to increase CRH secretion. CRH drives ACTH from the pituitary. ACTH drives cortisol from the adrenal cortex. The result: a chronically elevated baseline cortisol. Not a spike — a baseline elevation. Cortisol that doesn't follow a clean diurnal rhythm. Cortisol that is still measurably elevated in the evening when it should be at its lowest.

That elevated baseline cortisol suppresses melatonin onset, because cortisol and melatonin are inversely regulated through the same hypothalamic timing systems. It delays sleep. It reduces SWS depth by inhibiting growth hormone. And it fragments REM in the second half of the night by maintaining arousal threshold sensitivity when the system should be fully committed to dreaming sleep.

And here's the self-reinforcing part: poor sleep itself increases intestinal permeability, because cortisol has direct effects on the integrity of the gut epithelium. So the loop closes. Poor gut health generates poor sleep. Poor sleep degrades gut health. You're in a self-reinforcing feedback loop — the signature of a system locked into a dysfunctional, entrenched pattern.

Breaking the loop requires a direct dietary intervention.

Fibre first. Gut bacteria feed on indigestible carbohydrates — resistant starch, pectin, inulin, beta-glucan. A chronically low-fibre diet starves the microbiome. Diversity collapses. SCFA production falls. The target is thirty or more grams of fibre per day from diverse sources. Vegetables, legumes, whole grains, nuts, seeds, fruit. The diversity of fibre sources matters as much as the total — different bacterial species prefer different fibre types, so hitting your fibre grams from only oats and wheat isn't the same as hitting them from oats, lentils, broccoli, apples, and flaxseed.

Fermented foods. Yoghurt, kefir, sauerkraut, kimchi, miso. These introduce live bacterial cultures directly. A 2021 Stanford RCT — published in Cell, one of the most rigorous nutrition trials conducted — found that a high-fermented-food diet increased microbiome diversity and reduced inflammatory markers within ten weeks, and it outperformed a high-fibre diet alone on both metrics. Daily fermented food consumption is not a wellness trend. It is evidence-based microbiome support.

Prebiotic foods selectively feed beneficial bacterial species. Onion, garlic, leeks contain fructooligosaccharides. Green bananas and cooked-cooled potatoes contain resistant starch. Oats contain beta-glucan. Chicory root contains inulin. You do not need to buy supplements — these are common foods. Including one or two at each meal is sufficient to provide consistent prebiotic input.

What to reduce: ultra-processed foods contain emulsifiers — carboxymethylcellulose and polysorbate 80 are the most studied — that directly disrupt the intestinal mucus layer and impair tight junction integrity. This is the leaky gut mechanism from food additives, distinct from dietary deficiency. Excessive refined sugar feeds pathogenic species at the expense of beneficial ones. These are not minor variables. Transitioning away from ultra-processed food is one of the most direct gut-health interventions available.

Now — the cognitive performance dimension, because this is where it becomes directly relevant to WILD as a practice.

Lucid dreaming requires metacognition. It requires the ability to monitor your own mental state in real time, recognise incongruity, and maintain that recognition without collapsing back into the dream narrative. This is executive function under unusual conditions. REM sleep is the primary consolidation window for metacognitive and associative learning. The microbiome modulates BDNF, which is the molecular driver of synaptic plasticity during REM consolidation. A compromised gut microbiome doesn't just affect how long you sleep in REM — it affects the quality and efficiency of what REM sleep does to your cognitive architecture.

The gut is a key upstream variable. It is upstream of serotonin synthesis. It is upstream of cortisol regulation via the LPS-cytokine-HPA axis. It is upstream of BDNF and REM consolidation quality. Change gut microbiome diversity and multiple downstream sleep variables reorganise simultaneously. A single intervention with broad downstream effects — that is what makes the gut such a powerful primary leverage point in your sleep system.

You've been thinking about sleep as something that happens in your brain. Which it does. But the conditions for that sleep are partly manufactured in your gut, twenty-four hours a day, by organisms that outnumber your own cells. Treat that system accordingly.

## B5: Anti-Inflammatory Nutrition

*[Measured, scientific framing throughout. This is the "background noise" lesson — help the listener understand that inflammation is a silent, pervasive problem with a clear dietary solution. No alarmism; just clarity.]*

There is a category of physiological state that most clinical medicine ignores until it produces a diagnosable disease. It doesn't have a clean name. It's sometimes called metabolic endotoxaemia, sometimes chronic low-grade inflammation, sometimes just "inflamed." Whatever you call it, it describes a large portion of the modern adult population running at a background level of immune activation that produces no acute symptoms but degrades system performance across the board.

For sleep specifically — for the sleep architecture that underpins your WILD practice — chronic low-grade inflammation is one of the most prevalent and most correctable sources of system noise.

Here's the mechanism.

Pro-inflammatory cytokines — interleukin 1-beta, interleukin 6, TNF-alpha — are signalling molecules produced by immune cells in response to perceived threat. In acute infection, they do important work. But when they are chronically elevated at low levels, they have a specific effect on the HPA axis: they stimulate the hypothalamus to increase CRH, which drives cortisol, which creates the pattern we've seen in previous lessons — elevated baseline cortisol, blunted melatonin onset, suppressed SWS, fragmented REM.

Beyond the cortisol pathway, IL-1 beta and TNF-alpha cross the blood-brain barrier and directly modulate sleep architecture through independent central mechanisms. At very low levels, IL-1 beta actually supports NREM sleep — part of the immune system's resource reallocation strategy when the body is fighting infection. But at the chronically elevated low levels produced by a pro-inflammatory diet, the signal becomes noise. It disrupts the precise cycling between stages without clearly deepening any of them. The sleep is neither efficient nor architecturally clean.

Now — where does the inflammation come from?

Three primary dietary drivers.

Refined carbohydrates and added sugar. When blood glucose spikes after a high-glycaemic meal, it activates NF-κB — a transcription factor that drives production of inflammatory cytokines. Additionally, excess glucose produces AGEs: advanced glycation end-products, formed when glucose binds non-enzymatically to proteins. AGEs themselves stimulate inflammatory signalling. Every high-glycaemic meal generates a transient inflammatory response. When those meals are the daily norm, the response is no longer transient.

The omega-6 to omega-3 imbalance. Your cell membranes are built from fatty acids. The fatty acid profile of those membranes determines their signalling properties. Omega-6 fatty acids — linoleic acid, found in seed oils — are converted in your body to arachidonic acid, which is the precursor to pro-inflammatory eicosanoids: prostaglandins, leukotrienes. Omega-3 fatty acids — EPA and DHA — compete with arachidonic acid for the same enzymatic pathways and produce anti-inflammatory and pro-resolving mediators instead. The ideal dietary ratio of omega-6 to omega-3 is roughly 4:1. The modern Western diet, saturated with sunflower oil, safflower oil, corn oil, and soybean oil in nearly every processed food, runs at 15:1 to 20:1. That imbalance is pro-inflammatory by construction.

Ultra-processed foods as a category. Emulsifiers, food additives, refined carbohydrates, seed oils — ultra-processed foods combine multiple inflammatory drivers simultaneously. In large-scale epidemiological data, ultra-processed food consumption is consistently the strongest dietary predictor of CRP and IL-6 — the two primary blood markers of systemic inflammation. This is not one study. This is consistent across dozens of cohorts.

So what does the anti-inflammatory dietary pattern look like?

Omega-3 fatty acids. EPA and DHA, found in fatty fish — salmon, mackerel, sardines, herring. These are not passive anti-inflammatory agents. They are precursors to resolvins and protectins, signalling molecules that actively terminate inflammation rather than just inhibiting it. The research is solid: EPA/DHA supplementation at 1 to 3 grams per day reduces inflammatory markers and has been shown in controlled trials to improve sleep onset latency and REM duration. If you don't eat fatty fish two to three times per week, supplementation is warranted. Algae-based omega-3 is available for those who don't consume fish.

Polyphenols. These are plant compounds found in berries, dark chocolate above 70% cocoa, extra-virgin olive oil, green tea, turmeric, and colourful vegetables. They inhibit NF-κB signalling, reduce oxidative stress, and support the microbiome because many are prebiotic substrates. Curcumin specifically — the active polyphenol in turmeric — has been shown in RCTs to reduce IL-6 and TNF-alpha. Resveratrol, found in grapes and berries, activates SIRT1 pathways that are directly involved in circadian rhythm regulation. These are not supplement-only compounds — they are food compounds. Get them from food first, supplements second.

Replace refined carbohydrates, not carbohydrates. The target is not zero carbohydrate. It is replacing white flour, white sugar, and refined grains with whole food carbohydrate sources: legumes, sweet potato, oats, quinoa, root vegetables. The glycaemic response is lower, the NF-κB activation is reduced, and the fibre content supports the microbiome simultaneously.

The Mediterranean dietary pattern brings this together practically. High in vegetables, legumes, olive oil, fatty fish, whole grains, and nuts. Low in refined carbohydrates, processed meats, and seed oils. This pattern has been studied more extensively than any other dietary approach for both cardiovascular outcomes and cognitive performance. The PREDIMED studies — large RCTs involving thousands of participants — found that Mediterranean diet adherence was associated with better sleep duration, reduced insomnia symptoms, and higher sleep quality scores. The mechanisms are exactly those described above: reduced inflammation, improved omega-6:omega-3 ratio, increased fibre, increased polyphenol intake.

Inflammation is a source of physiological interference. Not because it is random, but because it consumes your body's regulatory capacity without contributing to any high-performance outcome. The HPA axis running in chronic low-grade activation, the autonomic nervous system maintaining a constant low-level alert response, the inflammatory pathways keeping immune cells primed — these all require metabolic and regulatory resources. That resource consumption is reflected directly in HRV suppression: a chronically inflamed system has lower HRV than a low-inflammation system with equivalent fitness, because the autonomic flexibility that HRV measures is being consumed by background immune and stress-response activity.

Removing interference is the foundational move. Anti-inflammatory dietary change is one of the most direct interventions available for doing that. You don't need to wait weeks to see the signal — HRV typically rises within seven to ten days of consistent anti-inflammatory dietary change, visible on any wearable with daily HRV tracking.

The practical floor is three changes. Replace cooking oils with extra-virgin olive oil. Add fatty fish twice a week or begin omega-3 supplementation. Remove ultra-processed foods as the dietary default. Those three changes alone move the omega-6:omega-3 ratio, reduce the primary NF-κB activators, and eliminate the most pro-inflammatory food additives. Do them for two weeks and read your HRV trend.

The sleep quality that becomes available when background inflammatory disruption is cleared is qualitatively different from medicated or supplemented sleep. The architecture is clean. The stages cycle properly. The REM windows are deep and extended. Everything else in this course works better on a low-noise substrate.

## B6: Exercise Timing and Sleep Architecture

*[Energised but precise — this is the exercise module, and it should feel like practical protocol delivery. No generic wellness framing; this is specific and mechanistic. Athletes and fitness-oriented practitioners are a subset of the audience.]*

Exercise makes you sleep better. You've heard that a thousand times. What you haven't heard — or at least haven't heard clearly — is why the timing of exercise matters as much as the exercise itself. And for a practice that depends on specific sleep architecture at specific times in the night, timing is the variable.

Let me start with the mechanism, because it explains everything.

Your core body temperature — CBT — is one of the primary biological clocks governing sleep onset. The transition from wakefulness into sleep requires a drop in core temperature of roughly half a degree to a full degree Celsius. This cooling is mechanistically required: it's part of the process that activates the GABAergic sleep-promoting neurons in the ventrolateral preoptic nucleus — the VLPO — that drive NREM initiation. If your core temperature hasn't dropped when you try to sleep, you get delayed sleep onset, lighter sleep in the first cycle, and compressed SWS.

Exercise raises core body temperature substantially — one to two degrees during the session, sustained for several hours afterward. Here's the insight: the magnitude of the evening CBT drop is partly determined by how high the morning peak was. A larger morning temperature rise creates a steeper diurnal curve. A steeper diurnal curve means a sharper, more complete evening decline. And a sharper evening decline means faster sleep onset and deeper SWS.

This is why morning exercise produces better sleep architecture than evening exercise, on average, across the literature. It's not that evening exercise is inherently disruptive — it's that morning exercise amplifies the thermal gradient that drives sleep onset. Studies on morning versus evening exercise timing consistently show morning exercisers achieving ten to twenty minutes faster sleep onset, ten to fifteen percent more SWS duration, and higher sleep efficiency. The mechanism is thermogenic, amplified by the circadian entrainment effect — morning light exposure combined with physical activity provides a double zeitgeber signal that sharply calibrates the day/night boundary.

Now — evening exercise, the nuances.

The popular rule is "don't exercise before bed." The evidence is more specific. High-intensity exercise — above seventy percent of VO2max — within three hours of sleep onset consistently delays sleep onset and fragments the first one to two sleep cycles. The mechanism involves sustained sympathetic activation through elevated catecholamines, core temperature that hasn't returned to baseline, and a post-exercise cortisol elevation that competes with melatonin onset. For a WILD practitioner who needs to be asleep within thirty minutes of going to bed to hit the right WBTB timing, delayed sleep onset from an evening HIIT session is a direct problem.

Moderate-intensity exercise in the evening — a brisk walk, light cycling, restorative yoga — is a different story. The evidence is equivocal: some studies show minor sleep onset delay, others show no effect or slight benefit through parasympathetic activation. The practical position is: keep anything above a walk to three hours or more before sleep. Below that threshold, light movement is fine and may actually be helpful for nervous system deactivation.

Resistance training deserves its own discussion. Heavy compound lifts — squats, deadlifts, rows, presses at seventy-five percent or more of one-rep max — are among the most potent stimuli for growth hormone secretion. GH is released primarily during the first slow-wave sleep episode of the night, in the large pulsatile spike that occurs approximately forty-five to ninety minutes after sleep onset. Resistance training in the morning or early afternoon primes this GH release window. It enhances the amplitude of the first SWS spike. Deeper early SWS means more efficient adenosine clearance in the first half of the night, which produces a larger, more consistent REM rebound in hours four through six.

This is the architecture you want for WILD. The first half of the night: deep, uninterrupted SWS with strong GH pulse. The second half: progressively longer REM episodes. Resistance training in the morning or early afternoon is one of the inputs that shapes this outcome.

Here's the protocol for WILD practitioners specifically.

Morning anchor, 6 to 9am. Twenty to forty-five minutes of aerobic exercise at moderate to high intensity. Running, cycling, swimming, circuit training. Outdoors is preferable — you get the light zeitgeber stacked on the exercise zeitgeber simultaneously. This is the non-negotiable session. It sets the thermal curve for the entire day.

Optional: resistance training in the early afternoon, roughly 2 to 5pm. This window allows post-exercise cortisol to clear before evening and positions the GH priming optimally. If you're using HIIT, this window also works — just keep intensity down from 5pm onward.

Optional evening mobility, 7 to 9pm. Stretching, yoga, light mobility work. This supports parasympathetic activation as you move toward sleep. It's a complement to the morning session, not a substitute.

WBTB night protocol. On a night when you intend to attempt WILD, no vigorous exercise after 5pm. On that morning, run your normal session. The morning session actually helps — it deepens the SWS in the first part of the night, which creates a cleaner, more defined transition into REM in the second half, which is exactly where your WBTB alarm is landing.

One caveat worth making explicit: exercise is a hormetic stressor. It expands physiological readiness through adaptive response. But only when recovery is adequate. Training volume or intensity that exceeds your recovery bandwidth produces the opposite of the intended effect — HRV suppression, elevated baseline cortisol, fragmented sleep, and a falling readiness score. This looks like over-training. Your wearable will show a falling 7-day HRV trend despite no other lifestyle changes.

The WILD programme is not a fitness programme. Exercise here is a tool for shaping sleep architecture, not an end in itself. Its dose should be calibrated to its effect on your physiological readiness metrics. If your HRV trend is falling over a week of maintained training load, reduce the load. Add a recovery day. Prioritise sleep. The training adaptation will follow the recovery, not the other way around.

Your body is not a machine you push harder to get more output. It is a system with recovery bandwidth. Respect the bandwidth and the system performs. Exceed the bandwidth consistently and the system degrades. Your morning HRV reading tells you which side of that line you're on.

## B7: Foundational Supplements

*[Calm, measured, slightly clinical — this is a precision module, not a sales module. The tone should communicate that every recommendation has a mechanism, a dose, and a reason. No enthusiasm for supplementation for its own sake.]*

I want to start with a frame that changes how supplements should be thought about entirely.

Most supplement marketing operates on a simple premise: more is better. Higher dose, stronger effect. The problem is that this is pharmacologically incorrect for nearly every sleep-relevant compound. Your sleep system is not a machine that needs to be pushed into a state. It is a complex physiological system that, under the right conditions, navigates to the states you want autonomously. The job of a supplement is not to override that system. It is to reduce the noise that's preventing the system from doing its own work.

Think of supplements as signal modulators. The question for any supplement is not "will this make me drowsy?" — sedation is not the target. The question is: does this compound reduce a specific source of interference in the system, or does it add interference while masking symptoms?

With that frame, here are the three foundational supplements, each with a specific mechanism and a specific rationale.

Magnesium glycinate.

Magnesium is the fourth most abundant mineral in the human body and a cofactor in over three hundred enzymatic reactions. For sleep specifically, two mechanisms are critical. First: GABA-A receptor potentiation. Magnesium acts as a positive allosteric modulator at GABA-A receptors — it enhances the inhibitory neurotransmission that drives NREM sleep. Low magnesium means reduced GABAergic tone, which manifests as lighter sleep, difficulty switching off cognitively, and more frequent micro-arousals. Second: NMDA receptor inhibition. Magnesium blocks NMDA glutamate receptors at physiological concentrations. Reduced NMDA activity means reduced neuronal hyperexcitability — less racing thoughts, less light sleep caused by excitatory runaway.

Magnesium deficiency is prevalent. Estimates put 45 to 68 percent of Western adults below the RDA from dietary sources — 320mg for women, 420mg for men. The primary food sources are green leafy vegetables, legumes, nuts, seeds, and whole grains — all foods that are systematically underconsumed in typical Western dietary patterns.

Form matters significantly. Magnesium oxide, the cheapest and most common supplement form, has approximately 4% bioavailability. Most of it goes straight through. Magnesium glycinate — magnesium chelated to the amino acid glycine — has substantially higher bioavailability and carries the additional benefit of glycine itself. Glycine is an inhibitory amino acid that lowers core body temperature by increasing peripheral blood flow and has independent RCT evidence for improving SWS duration and subjective sleep quality. The combination is synergistic. Magnesium citrate is a reasonable alternative — good bioavailability, though it has a mild laxative effect at higher doses.

Dose: 300 to 400mg magnesium glycinate, taken 45 to 60 minutes before bed. Start at 200mg and titrate up over one to two weeks to minimise digestive adjustment. Effects are typically noticeable within five to seven days, particularly in individuals who were deficient — which, statistically, is most people in the room.

L-theanine.

L-theanine is an amino acid found almost exclusively in green tea. It crosses the blood-brain barrier and its primary sleep-relevant effect is this: it increases alpha-wave activity in frontal and parietal cortex. Alpha waves are the 8 to 12 Hz oscillations associated with relaxed, attentive wakefulness. They are the dominant brainwave state at the hypnagogic threshold — the transition region between wakefulness and sleep where WILD entry occurs.

L-theanine does not sedate. This is important. A sedative drug pushes you through the hypnagogic state quickly and deposits you into NREM. That is the opposite of what you want for WILD. You want to slow down at the threshold, to linger in the alpha-theta boundary, to maintain enough awareness to catch the transition into dream imagery. L-theanine supports that by promoting the alpha state rather than suppressing it.

Practitioners who report difficulty with WILD entry fall into two categories. Those who fall asleep too quickly — their sleep pressure is too high and they collapse through the threshold. And those who remain too alert — they can't release into sleep. L-theanine helps both, but it's particularly useful for the second category. It induces the relaxed-but-aware state that the hypnagogic threshold requires.

Dose: 100 to 200mg taken 30 to 60 minutes before the WBTB return to sleep — not at initial sleep onset, but at the re-entry. This is where you want the alpha-wave effect: not to help you fall asleep at night, but to slow your descent at the threshold after your WBTB alarm. Take it when you wake for your WBTB, give it 20 to 30 minutes while you're gently conscious, and then return to bed.

L-theanine combines well with magnesium glycinate. They work on complementary pathways — GABA/NMDA versus alpha-wave promotion — and the combination is well-tolerated with no interaction concerns. No tolerance, no dependence, no side effects at doses up to 400mg.

Melatonin — the micro-dosing case.

Here is where I need to strongly contradict what most people believe about melatonin.

Melatonin is sold in 5mg and 10mg doses in most pharmacies. It is marketed as a sleep supplement, often alongside Benadryl and antihistamines. This is the wrong framing. Melatonin is not a sedative. It is a circadian signal — a hormonal message that says "it is dark, it is time to begin the physiological transition to sleep." It does not cause sleep. It times sleep.

Your body naturally produces approximately 0.1 to 0.3 milligrams of melatonin at night. A 5mg dose produces blood melatonin levels fifteen to fifty times above physiological range. At these levels, melatonin receptors downregulate within days. You become less sensitive to your own endogenous melatonin. The dose-response relationship inverts — multiple studies show worse sleep quality at 5-10mg than at 0.3-0.5mg. And the high dose persists in circulation well into the morning because melatonin's half-life is 45 to 60 minutes but its duration of action extends significantly beyond this, causing morning grogginess that most people attribute to "not being a morning person."

The correct use is micro-dosing.

For circadian phase adjustment — if your sleep is drifting late — 0.3mg at 9 to 10pm for five to seven days advances your circadian phase forward, making earlier sleep onset possible. This is backed by phase-response curve studies. It works. It is not sedating.

For REM extension via mid-night dosing — and this is the protocol most directly relevant to WILD practice — take 0.3 to 0.5mg at your WBTB wake time, before returning to sleep. Here is the mechanism: by 5am, endogenous melatonin is naturally declining. The pineal gland is winding down as the morning approaches. A small supplemental dose at the WBTB moment extends the melatonin signal — it maintains the "darkness is ongoing" message to the brain and, by doing so, extends REM propensity in the subsequent sleep period. You are prolonging the biological conditions for REM rather than forcing sedation. This is precision signal modulation — removing interference and supporting the system's own capacity to reach the target state.

One non-negotiable requirement: complete darkness during the subsequent sleep period. Any light exposure — including phone light, streetlight through thin curtains, a light from a hallway — will suppress melatonin regardless of the dose. A sleep mask is required for this protocol.

What to avoid.

High-dose melatonin above 1mg nightly. Explained above. If you are currently using high-dose melatonin, taper over two weeks to reach 0.3mg.

Antihistamines — diphenhydramine, doxylamine, found in Benadryl, Nytol, ZzzQuil, and most over-the-counter "sleep aids." These are first-generation H1 antagonists with strong anticholinergic properties. Acetylcholine is the primary neurotransmitter of REM sleep. The REM-on neurons in the brainstem require cholinergic tone to fire. Anticholinergic drugs suppress REM durably — effects persist for 24 to 48 hours after a single dose. A single night of antihistamine use can depress your REM architecture for two nights. For any REM-based practice, these compounds are incompatible.

Alcohol. Covered in B1. Mechanism: GABAergic sedation suppresses REM in the first half of the night, then fragmented REM rebound in the second. Not compatible with WILD practice within four to five hours of sleep onset.

Benzodiazepines and Z-drugs — zolpidem, temazepam, diazepam. These produce pharmacological sedation that is architecturally distinct from natural sleep. They suppress both SWS and REM and produce tolerance rapidly. If you are prescribed these, consult your doctor before any changes. Do not self-discontinue.

The stack. Magnesium glycinate 300-400mg with dinner or before bed. L-theanine 100-200mg at WBTB re-entry. Melatonin 0.3mg at the same WBTB moment if you're doing mid-night REM extension. That is the foundational Body track supplement protocol. Simple, evidence-based, and mechanistically targeted. Nothing here creates dependence. Nothing here sedates. These are signal modulators. Used correctly, they reduce noise and support the system's own capacity to produce the architecture you need.

## B8: HRV and Sleep Tracking — Your Readiness Score

*[This is the synthesis module — calm, considered, data-oriented. The practitioner who has worked through B1-B7 should feel, by the end of this script, that they have a complete measurement system for everything they've been doing. The tone is confident but not clinical-cold — this is genuinely useful practical infrastructure.]*

Everything in this Body track — the nutrition timing, the glucose management, the gut work, the exercise protocol, the supplements — produces effects that ultimately show up as changes in your physiology. The question is: where do you read those changes? How do you know whether what you're doing is working? And more importantly: how do you know, on any given night, whether your system is in a state from which the practice can succeed?

The answer is your nightly readiness score. Three variables, a single score, a binary decision.

Let me start with the core metric: HRV.

Heart rate variability is the variation in time between consecutive heartbeats. Measured in milliseconds. Your heart does not beat like a metronome — the intervals vary from beat to beat, and that variation is regulated by the autonomic nervous system. Specifically by the balance between the sympathetic branch — activating, alert, preparing for action — and the parasympathetic branch — recovering, restoring, maintaining reserve.

High HRV means the parasympathetic system is dominant and the autonomic system has flexibility. The interval between beats varies widely because the vagus nerve is modulating the heart rate dynamically, second by second. Low HRV means sympathetic dominance. The autonomic system is under load — recovering from a stressor, running at a chronic sub-threshold alert level, or in an impaired state.

For sleep: parasympathetic dominance is a prerequisite for slow-wave sleep initiation. The sleep-promoting neurons in the ventrolateral preoptic nucleus are activated under conditions of low arousal and parasympathetic tone. High HRV before bed correlates directly with faster sleep onset, deeper SWS in the first half of the night, and higher sleep efficiency. Studies correlating pre-sleep HRV with subsequent sleep architecture find correlations of 0.6 to 0.7 between HRV and SWS duration — stronger than any subjective sleepiness measure and comparable to predictions made from laboratory polysomnography.

High HRV tells you: the system is ready. Low HRV tells you: the system is under load. Attempt a demanding sleep architecture practice when the system is under load, and you are trying to drive to a state the system currently cannot reach.

Reading the data correctly: trends, not single nights.

Individual HRV readings are noisy. They vary with body position during measurement, breathing pattern, meal timing that day, ambient temperature, the timing of your last training session, psychological stress in the past 24 hours. A single reading is information; a trend is intelligence.

Read your HRV as a seven-day rolling average. What you're tracking is: trend direction and deviation from your personal baseline. A rising seven-day average means recovery is outpacing stress input — control capacity is being built. A falling seven-day average means accumulated load is exceeding recovery — control capacity is being consumed.

Your baseline is personal. It is determined by your genetics, age, fitness level, and training history. A 35-year-old recreational athlete might run at 50 to 60ms. A competitive endurance athlete might run at 80 to 100ms. Comparing yourself to population norms is not useful. What matters is: how does today's reading compare to your own 30-day rolling average? A morning reading more than 10% below your personal baseline is a low-readiness signal regardless of what the absolute number is.

After a hard resistance training session or HIIT session, expect HRV to dip 5 to 10% in the following 24 to 48 hours and recover within 48 to 72. That is the hormetic cycle — stress, dip, adapt, recover higher. HRV that fails to return to baseline within 72 hours after a training session is a sign that recovery bandwidth is insufficient. Reduce training load, increase sleep duration, address nutrition.

Now — the score. Three variables, scored -1 to +1 each.

Variable one: HRV. If your seven-day HRV average is above your personal baseline, score +1. At baseline plus or minus 5%, score 0. More than 5% below baseline, score -1.

Variable two: glucose variability from the prior night. If you have a CGM: stable overnight trace with less than 2 millimole variation, score +1. Moderate variation 2 to 3 millimoles, score 0. High variation above 3 millimoles or a trough below 4.0, score -1. If you don't have a CGM — most people won't — use nocturnal waking events as proxy: no spontaneous waking, score +1. One brief waking, score 0. Multiple wakings or a 3am arousal, score -1.

Variable three: sleep regularity over the prior seven days. Sleep onset within 30 minutes of your target time every night for seven days, score +1. Within 30 minutes for five or six of seven nights, score 0. Variable by more than 60 minutes on more than two nights, score -1.

Sum the three scores. Your readiness score for tonight.

Plus three: highest priority WILD night. Every factor aligned. Deploy advanced protocols — WBTB plus MILD plus SSILD or WILD combined entry. This is the night to attempt demanding technique work.

Plus one or plus two: ready. Standard WILD practice. Above-average conditions. Run your normal protocol.

Zero: marginal. Basic technique only. Use simple MILD visualisation rather than full WILD technique. Expect below-average returns but don't waste the window entirely.

Minus one to minus three: recovery night. This is a hard rule. No active WILD attempt. No alarm. Let the night be what the night needs to be — uninterrupted recovery. The protocol: standard supplement stack, dinner by 7pm, no screens in the final hour, 4-7-8 breathing for four rounds before sleep (inhale four counts, hold seven, exhale eight — this is a direct vagal activation technique that measurably improves the following morning's HRV), and optionally a page of written reflection to externalise whatever is generating cognitive arousal and HPA activation.

The recovery night is not wasted. This is the most important thing I want you to take from this lesson. Every high-readiness night is built on the recovery nights that produced it. A practitioner who attempts the technique regardless of readiness score is not demonstrating commitment. They are demonstrating a failure to read the system's signals — and those repeated low-readiness attempts are counterproductive, because they fragment recovery sleep that would have built the capacity for a successful future attempt.

The wearable options.

Oura Ring Generation 3 is currently the most accurate consumer wearable for overnight HRV and sleep staging. The ring form factor sits at the finger, close to an artery, with minimal motion artefact. The readiness score algorithm has been independently validated against polysomnography. It reports HRV, respiratory rate, skin temperature, sleep stage distribution, and a composite readiness score. Recommended if readiness tracking is your primary use case.

WHOOP 4.0 tracks HRV continuously, not just overnight. The recovery score and strain metric give you a training-load-versus-recovery balance that is particularly useful if you're running the exercise protocol from B6 actively. No display — data goes to the phone only. Good for practitioners who want no light stimulus at night.

Garmin — Fenix, Epix, Forerunner 955 and above — tracks HRV status with a 7-day baseline comparison built into the platform. The Body Battery metric is a simplified HRV-derived readiness score. Less granular than Oura or WHOOP but sufficient for readiness tracking if you're already using Garmin for sport.

Important caveat on all of them: consumer wearables use photoplethysmography — optical sensing at the skin surface — to estimate HRV. This is less accurate than ECG for absolute values, particularly at high HRV. But for trend tracking and baseline deviation, the consistency of the device is what matters. And consumer devices are consistent enough for the comparative analysis your nightly readiness score requires.

The thirty-day onboarding. Run the readiness log for thirty days. At the end, correlate your daily scores with your WILD practice outcomes. Most practitioners find by day twenty that the correlation is visible and strong enough to identify their personal readiness threshold — the minimum score at which their attempts succeed. Make that threshold a hard rule. Below it, recovery protocol. Above it, practice.

This is not a complex system. Three columns in a spreadsheet, scored nightly, producing a single number that determines one decision. But the decision it enables is the most operationally important decision in this practice: tonight, or not tonight?

Trust the data. The system is telling you something. Read it correctly and act accordingly. The nights you protect as recovery nights are the nights that make the practice nights work.
